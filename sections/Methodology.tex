\section{A Tokenomics-Driven Ecosystem for Agent Society}
\label{sec:ecosystem}

This section outlines the concrete design of our tokenomics-driven decentralized ecosystem for the agent society. We first formalize the agent roles and knowledge service abstractions that form the architecture's foundation (\S\ref{sec:motivation}). Building on this basis, we describe the core interaction primitives, namely promotion, subscription, and validation, through which agents engage with one another (\S\ref{sec:mechanism}). Finally, we detail the tokenomics incentive structure, incorporating staking, rewards, and penalties, to ensure the entire ecosystem remains self-enforcing and economically sustainable (\S\ref{sec:tokenomics_loop}).


\subsection{Agent Roles and Agent Services}
\label{sec:motivation}

In the agent society, specialized intelligence is distributed across a large-scale, open population of autonomous agents, where each agent possesses domain-specific intelligence composed of its trained knowledge, reasoning capabilities, and retrieval resources, and operates under the authority of its deploying organization. When an agent encounters a task outside its own competence, it must locate and engage a capable counterpart, a process we term agent service routing. Rather than delegating this task to a centralized orchestrator, our ecosystem allows routing to emerge organically from token-mediated economic interactions among three dynamically interchangeable roles.

For any agent $u \in \mathcal{U}$ with domain-specific expertise $\mathcal{K}_u$, the most immediate roles arise from the service supply-demand structure.

% In the agent society, specialized knowledge is distributed across a large, open population of autonomous agents $\mathcal{U}$, each possessing domain-specific expertise $\mathcal{K}_u$ and operating under the authority of its deploying organization. When an agent encounters a task that falls outside its own competence, it must locate and engage a capable counterpart---a process we term \textit{service routing}. Rather than delegating this routing to a centralized orchestrator, our ecosystem allows it to emerge organically from token-mediated economic interactions among three dynamically interchangeable roles.

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Consumer Agent)}}\label{def:consumer}
A consumer $c \in \mathcal{U}$ is an agent that requests services from the ecosystem. Upon encountering a task outside its domain expertise, it identifies the required expertise domain and issues a set of domain-specific tasks $\{x_1, \ldots, x_{N}\} \subset \mathcal{Q}$ to be delegated externally. A consumer agent seeks to establish a long-term service agreement with a qualified producer agent, committing tokens to fund this engagement. Unlike a human user who subjectively "asks a question," the consumer agent executes a deterministic routing protocol driven by its utility function.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Producer Agent)}}\label{def:producer}
A producer $p \in \mathcal{U}$ is an agent that provides agent services by encapsulating its domain expertise $\mathcal{K}_p$ into a callable inference capability. Given a task $x \in \mathcal{Q}$, the producer executes its service $a_p(x)$ and returns an output $r \in \mathcal{R}$. The service quality $q_p \in (0,1]$, representing the probability of generating a correct output, is treated as an objective property of the agent that can be estimated through sampling. By delivering high-quality outputs, the producer agent earns tokens from the consumer agent.
\end{definition}

As established in \Cref{sec:trust_crisis}, the consumer-producer dyad alone cannot sustain a reliable agent service market. The information asymmetry between supply and demand, where the consumer lacks the means to verify service quality and the producer may overstate its competence, triggers the adverse selection that our mechanism must overcome. To break this asymmetry and move beyond the risks of centralized "black-box" routing, we introduce a third role: validators, independent agents that provide decentralized quality assessments of agent service on behalf of the ecosystem.

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Validator Agent)}}\label{def:validator}
A validator $v \in \mathcal{U}$ is an independent agent that performs cross-verification within the agent service routing process. Given a task $x \in \mathcal{Q}$ and a producer's output $a_p(x)$, the validator agent independently executes its own inference $a_v(x)$ to produce a judgment $j_v \in \{0, 1\}$, indicating agreement or disagreement. This is an auditable computational process rather than a subjective evaluation. To enhance reliability, judgments from multiple validators can be aggregated to form a consensus that ensures the integrity of the routing results (see \S\ref{sec:poi_detail}).
\end{definition}

Central to both roles is the concept of domain-specific and intelligence-intensive agent services. A producer contributes to the ecosystem through expected long-term service engagements by fulfilling recurring consumer requests, while a validator supports the agent service routing process by independently assessing the quality of producer-consumer matching beforehand. Although their purposes differ, one generating responses and the other verifying them, both agents rely on the same underlying inference capability to provide their respective services.

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Agent Service Routing)}}\label{def:agent-service-routing}
Each validator $v \in \mathcal{U}$ encapsulates its domain expertise $\mathcal{K}_v$ into a knowledge-intensive routing service $a_v: \mathcal{Q} \rightarrow \mathcal{R}$, providing a reference inference to determine a producer's reliability. From its own perspective, the validator utilizes $a_v$ to assess a producer's performance on a specific task, where $q \in (0,1]$ reflects its internal confidence in the verification's accuracy. This process incurs a computational cost $c_v$ per invocation. Ultimately, the validator functions as a decentralized benchmark, leveraging its proprietary inference pipeline to participate in the service routing between consumers and producers.
\end{definition}

When $u$ acts as a producer, $a_u$ functions as the primary solution, delivering direct utility to the consumer by resolving their specific task. Conversely, when $u$ acts as a validator, the same $a_u$ serves as an independent benchmark, not to solve the consumer's problem directly, but to provide an epistemic reference for assessing the producer’s performance.

Conceptually, an agent’s inference capability constitutes a non-transferable asset inherently bound to its owner, shaped by its unique training data, reasoning architecture, and retrieval resources. This service quality represents the agent’s core competitive advantage, dictating both its earning potential as a producer and its credibility as a validator. Specifically, this capability exhibits a functional duality defined by its operational context: as a producer, the agent employs its expertise as the primary solution to deliver direct utility to the consumer; conversely, as a validator, it utilizes the same service as an independent benchmark to provide an epistemic reference for assessing performance. Within this two-sided market, agent intelligence is the traded commodity, while tokens serve as the medium of exchange that aligns individual incentives with ecosystem-wide welfare.

\begin{insightbox}{Agent Roles in the Ecosystem}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=3pt, topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Consumer agent} --- identifies tasks beyond its expertise and delegates them externally via token-backed service requests.
    \item \textbf{Producer agent} --- encapsulates domain expertise into a callable agent service $a_p$, expecting to earn tokens for continuous reliable outputs.
    \item \textbf{Validator agent} --- provides an independent routing service $a_v$ that benchmarks the producer’s output, contributing a judgment to the ecosystem’s consensus.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{1pt}
\textit{Role Fluidity}: Any agent may assume any role based on its context and expertise. The same agent may act as a producer in one domain, a consumer in another, and a validator in a third.
\end{insightbox}

\subsubsection{Promotion and Subscription}

The agent service lifecycle revolves around two complementary economic actions that together constitute the two-sided market at the heart of the ecosystem. Producers promote their agent services to establish visibility and attract potential users, while consumers subscribe to preferred producers for long-term agent service. These two workflows are illustrated in Figures~\ref{fig:promotion} and~\ref{fig:subscription}.

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Promotion)}}\label{def:promotion}
A producer agent $p$ initiates a promotion by advertising its service capabilities and staking a commitment fee $\tau_p$. This stake functions as a costly signal, ensuring that only agents with high confidence in their competence will risk the potential forfeiture of tokens to establish market visibility.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Subscription)}}\label{def:subscription}
A consumer agent $c$ establishes a \textit{subscription} to a producer's service $a_p$ by paying a fee $\tau_s$ in tokens, forming a service-level agreement (SLA) between the two autonomous agents. Under this agreement, the consumer delegates future tasks to $a_p$, and the producer becomes obligated to respond within specified quality and latency parameters. 
\end{definition}

\begin{insightbox}{Agent Service Lifecycle at a Glance}
\textbf{Promotion} (supply side): Producer stakes $\tau_p$ $\to$ requests validator assessment $\to$ if consensus is favorable, offers service to consumer.\\[3pt]
\textbf{Subscription} (demand side): Consumer optionally requests independent validation $\to$ if satisfied, commits $\tau_s$ and establishes SLA with producer.\\[3pt]
\textit{Key property}: Both actions are high-stakes, token-backed commitments, creating a natural demand for third-party validation before either party commits.
\end{insightbox}

In practice, promotion and subscription are high-stakes, long-term commitments involving substantial token expenditure. Because poor matches erode trust and undermine incentive alignment, rational agents have a strong incentive to seek independent quality assessments before finalizing any agreement. This demand for transparency and reliability is precisely what the validation protocol addresses.

In conventional systems, producer-consumer agent routing relies heavily on subjective judgment, where producers evaluate their own competence and consumers rely on intuition to judge results. However, objective ground truth is often unavailable because consumers usually lack the expertise to verify correctness, while producers may operate based on partial or biased knowledge. To bridge this gap, the ecosystem provides a structured third-party validation mechanism that both producers and consumers can request before committing to high-stakes transactions.

% Given that agent service quality cannot be unilaterally verified by the consumer, the ecosystem provides a structured third-party validation mechanism that both producers and consumers can invoke before committing to high-stakes transactions. By design, the total cost of validation ($m \times n \times \tau_{\text{val}}$) is substantially lower than that of a mismatched subscription ($\tau_s$), making the request for validation economically rational for both parties. The protocol operates in two stages, each employing an independent validator committee to ensure integrity.

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Promotion Validation Request)}}\label{def:promo_validation}
When a consumer agent $c$ broadcasts a service need along with a sample task set $\mathcal{Q}_c \subset \mathcal{Q}$ ($|\mathcal{Q}_c| = m$), a producer agent $p$ requests a group of validators $V_p \subset \mathcal{U}$ ($|V_p| = n$) and pays each validator a fixed fee $\tau_{\text{val}}$ per task to assess its service capability before initiating a formal promotion.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Subscription Validation Request)}}\label{def:sub_validation}
Upon receiving a promotion, the consumer agent $c$ issues a separate task set $\mathcal{Q}_s \subset \mathcal{Q}$ ($|\mathcal{Q}_s| = m$) to a disjoint group of validators $V_s \subset \mathcal{U}$ ($|V_s| = n$), subject to the constraints $\mathcal{Q}_s \cap \mathcal{Q}_c = \varnothing$ and $V_s \cap V_p = \varnothing$. Each validator in $V_s$ is paid the same per-task fee $\tau_{\text{val}}$ to independently assess the producer's service quality.
\end{definition}

By design, the total cost of validation $m \cdot n \cdot \tau_{\text{val}}$ is substantially lower than the potential loss from a mismatched subscription $\tau_s$, where $m$ and $n$ denote the sizes of the task set $\mathcal{Q}_c$ (or $\mathcal{Q}_s$) and the validator committee $V_p$ (or $V_s$) respectively. Meanwhile, the per-task fee is structured to exceed each validator’s computational cost $c_v < \tau_{\text{val}}$. This makes validation a cost-efficient safeguard: rational agents are incentivized to invoke the protocol because the modest upfront expenditure mitigates the far greater risks of biased self-assessment and long-term commitment failure. To maintain systemic integrity, the protocol operates in two distinct stages, each employing an independent validator committee to prevent collusion and ensure objectivity.1.

\subsubsection{Consensus Formation and Proof-of-Intelligence}
\label{sec:poi_detail}

To improve the transparency and auditability of agent service routing, our ecosystem employs a decentralized consensus formation process in which heterogeneous validator agents collaborate to deliver a more reliable judgment than any individual could provide. Once a validation request is issued, the selected committee serves as a decentralized proxy to assess producer-consumer alignment: each validator independently invokes its routing service and checks whether its own output concurs with that of the producer. The resulting individual judgments are aggregated through majority voting into a single, auditable consensus signal, which is returned to the requester---the producer in promotion validation, or the consumer in subscription validation---as a reference for the final routing decision. We formalize this process for both stages below.

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Promotion Consensus)}}\label{def:promo_consensus}
Let $V_p = \{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ denote the validator committee for promotion. Each validator $v_i \in V_p$ independently invokes its routing service to produce a judgment $r_i = a_i(\mathcal{Q}_c, a_p)$ by comparing its own outputs with those of the candidate producer on the sample task set $\mathcal{Q}_c$. A promotion consensus $r^*$ is derived through majority voting:
\begin{equation}
r^* = \textup{MajorityVote}\left(\{r_1, \dots, r_n\}\right).
\end{equation}
The producer may adopt $r^*$ as a reference signal to decide whether to proceed with the promotion offer.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Subscription Consensus)}}\label{def:sub_consensus}
Let $V_s = \{v_1', \ldots, v_n'\}$ denote the disjoint validator committee for subscription. Each validator $v_j' \in V_s$ independently invokes its routing service to produce a judgment $r_j' = a_j(\mathcal{Q}_s, a_p)$ by comparing its own outputs with those of the producer on the separate task set $\mathcal{Q}_s$. A subscription consensus $r^{**}$ is reached by majority voting:
\begin{equation}
r^{**} = \textup{MajorityVote}\left(\{r_1', \ldots, r_n'\}\right).
\end{equation}
The consumer may use $r^{**}$ as a reference signal to decide whether the producer's service is sufficiently aligned with its domain requirements.
\end{definition}

We refer to this consensus formation process---validator-mediated, majority-vote, autonomy-preserving---as the \textbf{Proof-of-Intelligence (PoI)} paradigm introduced in \Cref{sec:vision}. Where Proof-of-Work~\cite{jakobsson1999proofs} demands raw computation and Proof-of-Stake~\cite{dwork1992pricing} demands financial capital, PoI demands what matters most in the agent society: \textit{domain-specific expertise and verifiable intellectual effort}. By delegating quality assessment to a committee of economically motivated validator agents, PoI transforms a problem that no individual consumer or centralized orchestrator could resolve into one that the ecosystem addresses collectively, through incentive-aligned collaboration among distributed intelligences.

For PoI to yield reliable judgments, however, the validator committee must satisfy a critical prerequisite that we call \textit{agent heterogeneity}. This heterogeneity operates at multiple levels: validators should differ not only in their underlying model architectures (e.g., a Llama-based validator assessing a GPT-based producer), but also in the training data, retrieval resources, and reasoning configurations that together constitute their domain expertise. Validators sharing similar expertise would exhibit highly correlated errors, reducing majority voting to a single point of failure. By enforcing heterogeneity across the committee, the ecosystem approximates the statistical independence assumption that underpins both the Condorcet jury theorem and the game-theoretic analysis in \Cref{sec:game_theory}, converting an abstract mathematical condition into a concrete, enforceable protocol rule.


\subsection{Tokenomics Incentive Structure}
\label{sec:tokenomics_loop}

The long-term viability of the agent ecosystem hinges on a carefully calibrated tokenomics incentive structure, encompassing staking, rewards, and penalties across all three agent roles, that renders honest, effortful participation the dominant economic strategy for every participant. We first detail the token-based mechanisms that govern the economic consequences of each agent's actions, and then describe how these token flows aggregate into the self-reinforcing dual loop that sustains the ecosystem as a whole.

\subsubsection{Staking, Rewards, and Penalties}

Each validator agent participating in a validation round must lock a fixed amount of tokens $\sigma$ as an economic commitment, creating direct financial exposure that deters dishonest behavior. Once voting concludes, a validator is rewarded if its judgment aligns with the consensus ($r_i = r^*$ in promotion or $r_j' = r^{**}$ in subscription), and penalized otherwise. The total reward pool consists of two sources: the requester's validation payment ($mn\tau_{\text{val}}$) and all validators' stakes ($n\sigma$). Letting $n_{\text{agree}}$ denote the number of validators whose judgments agree with the consensus, the per-capita payoff is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:validator_payoff_mechanism}
U_v =
\begin{cases}
\displaystyle\frac{mn\tau_{\text{val}} + n\sigma}{n_{\text{agree}}} - \sigma & \text{if } r_v = r^* \text{ (agree with consensus)}, \\[8pt]
-\sigma & \text{if } r_v \neq r^* \text{ (disagree with consensus)}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
In the conservative case where all $n$ validators agree ($n_{\text{agree}} = n$), the per-capita reward reduces to $R_{\min} = m\tau_{\text{val}} + \sigma$, which is the lower bound used in our game-theoretic analysis (\Cref{sec:ic}). This staking-and-reward structure gives rise to several important properties of the ecosystem:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=4pt]
\item \textbf{Sybil defense.} The staking requirement directly addresses the accountability crisis identified in \Cref{sec:trust_crisis}: while deploying a new agent is virtually free, staking tokens on that agent is not. An attacker seeking to dominate the consensus with $k$ Sybil validators must lock $k\sigma$ tokens---an investment that scales linearly with the attack's scope and is forfeited upon failure, effectively replacing disposable identity with costly economic commitment.

\item \textbf{Collusion resistance.} Strategic validator selection is mitigated by the disjoint committee design ($V_s \cap V_p = \varnothing$): even if the producer selects favorable validators for promotion, the consumer independently re-validates with a separate committee before committing to subscription.

\item \textbf{Moral hazard.} Despite the staking mechanism, validators face a persistent temptation to \textit{shirk}---returning random judgments at zero cost rather than exerting genuine effort at cost $c_u$. Resolving this moral hazard requires formal proof that truthful effort is each validator's optimal strategy, which we provide in \Cref{sec:ic}.

\item \textbf{Utility-based acceptance.} The requesting agent does not blindly follow the consensus: producers promote and consumers subscribe only when their expected utility exceeds the token-backed commitment, yielding threshold-optimal policies formalized in \Cref{sec:producer_ir,sec:consumer_ir}.
\end{itemize}

Taken together, the validation protocol of \Cref{sec:poi_detail} and the tokenomics structure introduced above define the complete validation lifecycle that constitutes Proof-of-Intelligence in operation:

\begin{insightbox}{Validation Lifecycle (Proof-of-Intelligence)}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=1pt, topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Stake}: Each validator agent locks $\sigma$ tokens as a financial commitment to the ecosystem.
    \item \textbf{Infer}: Each validator independently executes its routing service $a_v(x)$ on the sample tasks, drawing on its own expertise.
    \item \textbf{Compare}: Each validator compares its output against the producer's output $a_p(x)$, producing a binary judgment $r_v \in \{0,1\}$.
    \item \textbf{Vote}: Individual judgments are aggregated via majority voting to form consensus $r^*$.
    \item \textbf{Settle}: Validators aligned with consensus receive rewards from the shared pool; those who deviate forfeit their stake.
\end{enumerate}
\end{insightbox}


\subsubsection{The Tokenomics--Knowledge Dual Loop}

Viewed at the ecosystem level, the token flows across all roles and transactions form a self-reinforcing cycle---the \textbf{tokenomics--knowledge dual loop} illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:tokenomics}. The loop operates through a simple but powerful feedback mechanism: service quality drives token rewards (producers earn subscription fees for reliable outputs; validators earn validation fees for truthful assessments), and token accumulation in turn expands each agent's participation opportunities, creating a virtuous cycle that continuously incentivizes honest engagement and the refinement of each agent's intelligence. Conversely, agents that deliver low-quality outputs or shirk effort see their token reserves deplete through failed validations and forfeited stakes, and are progressively filtered out.

This dual loop constitutes the primary driving force behind the long-term sustainability and evolution of the decentralized agent ecosystem, sustaining cooperation, accountability, and quality verification as emergent properties of rational self-interest rather than external mandates. A critical enabler is that LLM agents---unlike human participants who may exhibit bounded rationality---are programmatic utility maximizers whose behavior can be precisely modeled as optimal responses to incentive structures, making the game-theoretic guarantees established in \Cref{sec:game_theory} particularly well-suited to the agent society context.


\subsubsection{Protocol Summary}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:ecosystem} consolidates the complete agent service engagement lifecycle---from initial need identification, through validator-mediated quality assessment, to confirmed subscription---into a single pseudocode specification.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Agent Service Routing Protocol}
\label{alg:ecosystem}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Statex \textbf{Phase 1: Service Need Identification}
\State Consumer $c$ encounters task outside its expertise
\State $c$ broadcasts sample task set $\mathcal{Q}_c$ with $|\mathcal{Q}_c| = m$
\Statex
\Statex \textbf{Phase 2: Promotion with Validation} \Comment{Producer-initiated}
\State Producer $p$ stakes promotion fee $\tau_p$
\State $p$ selects validator committee $V_p$ ($|V_p| = n$) and pays each validator $\tau_{\text{val}}$ per task
\For{each $v_i \in V_p$ \textbf{in parallel}}
    \State $v_i$ stakes $\sigma$, executes $a_{v_i}(x)$ for $x \in \mathcal{Q}_c$, produces $r_i \in \{0,1\}$
\EndFor
\State $r^* \gets \text{MajorityVote}(\{r_1, \dots, r_n\})$
\State Settle: aligned validators share reward pool; deviators forfeit $\sigma$
\If{$r^* = 1$ \textbf{and} expected payoff justifies promotion}
    \State $p$ sends promotion offer to $c$
\EndIf
\Statex
\Statex \textbf{Phase 3: Subscription with Validation} \Comment{Consumer-initiated}
\State $c$ issues fresh tasks $\mathcal{Q}_s$ ($\mathcal{Q}_s \cap \mathcal{Q}_c = \varnothing$)
\State $c$ selects disjoint committee $V_s$ ($V_s \cap V_p = \varnothing$), pays each validator $\tau_{\text{val}}$ per task
\For{each $v_j' \in V_s$ \textbf{in parallel}}
    \State $v_j'$ stakes $\sigma$, executes $a_{v_j'}(x)$, produces $r_j' \in \{0,1\}$
\EndFor
\State $r^{**} \gets \text{MajorityVote}(\{r_1', \dots, r_n'\})$
\State Settle: aligned validators share reward pool; deviators forfeit $\sigma$
\If{$r^{**} = 1$ \textbf{and} expected utility justifies subscription}
    \State $c$ commits subscription fee $\tau_s$ and begins long-term service engagement with $p$
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


\begin{remarkbox}{Implementation Considerations}
\label{sec:decentral_tech}
The mechanism design above is infrastructure-agnostic in principle, but its trustless execution naturally maps onto blockchain-based infrastructure. In the agent society, where participants are deployed by independent, competing organizations with no pre-existing trust relationships, no single entity can serve as a credible neutral party. A shared ledger enforced by network consensus provides the necessary trust layer for token management, stake escrow, and automated reward distribution via smart contracts, while a hybrid on-chain/off-chain architecture preserves the data sovereignty that cross-organizational agents require (protocol state on-chain; proprietary knowledge assets off-chain with cryptographic integrity proofs). We leave the full system implementation as future work and focus this paper on the mechanism design, incentive analysis, and empirical validation.
\end{remarkbox}
