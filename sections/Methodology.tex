\section{A Decentralized Ecosystem for LLM Agents}

Having identified the trust crisis in agent collaboration and argued for a decentralized, incentive-driven paradigm in Section~1, we now present the concrete design of our ecosystem.
We begin by formalizing the ecosystem's role structure and motivating each design choice from the challenges identified previously (\S\ref{sec:motivation}).
We then detail the complete mechanism design---including the transaction protocol, validation workflow, Proof-of-Intelligence, and incentive structure (\S\ref{sec:mechanism}).
Finally, we describe the decentralized infrastructure that enables trustless execution (\S\ref{sec:decentral_tech}).


\subsection{Ecosystem Architecture}
\label{sec:motivation}

\subsubsection{From Knowledge Heterogeneity to Role Specialization}

The agent society introduced in Section~1 is characterized by a large, open population of agents $\mathcal{U}$, each possessing domain-specific knowledge $\mathcal{K}_u$ and operating under the authority of its deploying organization.
When an agent encounters a task outside its domain expertise, it must locate and engage a capable counterpart---a process we term \textit{service routing}.
In the centralized paradigm, an orchestrator manages this routing; in our ecosystem, routing emerges from decentralized economic interactions among three roles.

The first two roles arise directly from the knowledge supply-demand structure:

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Consumer)}}\label{def:consumer}
A consumer $c \in \mathcal{U}$ is an agent that performs \textit{computational need identification}: upon encountering a task outside its domain expertise, it decomposes the problem, identifies the required knowledge domain, and issues a set of domain-specific tasks $\mathcal{Q} = \{q_1, \cdots ,q_{N}\}$ to be delegated externally. The consumer seeks to establish a long-term service agreement with a qualified producer $p \in \mathcal{U}$, committing tokens to fund this engagement. Unlike a human user who subjectively ``asks a question,'' the consumer agent executes a deterministic routing protocol driven by its utility function.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Producer)}}\label{def:producer}
A producer $p \in \mathcal{U}$ is an agent that encapsulates domain knowledge $\mathcal{K}_p$ into a callable \textit{inference capability}: given a task $q \in \mathcal{Q}$, it executes its service $a_p(q)$ and returns an output $r \in \mathcal{R}$. The producer's service quality $q_p \in (0,1]$---the probability of generating a correct output---is an objective, measurable property that can be estimated through sampling, rather than a subjective reputation score. By delivering reliable, high-quality outputs, the producer earns tokens from the consumer.
\end{definition}

However, as established in Section~1.2, the consumer-producer dyad alone cannot sustain a reliable market: the information asymmetry between knowledge supply and demand---where consumers cannot verify producer quality, and producers may overstate their capabilities---triggers adverse selection that can unravel the entire ecosystem.

\subsubsection{Validators as the Missing Trust Layer}

To break this information asymmetry, we introduce a third role that is structurally absent from existing multi-agent frameworks: \textbf{validators}---independent agents who provide decentralized quality assessment on behalf of requesters.

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Validator)}}\label{def:validator}
A validator $v \in \mathcal{U}$ is an independent agent that performs \textit{cross-verification}: given a task $q \in \mathcal{Q}$ and a producer $p$'s output $a_p(q)$, the validator independently executes its own inference $a_v(q)$ and compares the two outputs to produce a binary judgment $r_v \in \{0, 1\}$ (agree/disagree). This is not a subjective evaluation but an \textit{auditable computational process}---the validator's reasoning trace and output can be recorded and verified. Multiple validators' judgments are aggregated via majority voting to form a consensus that is strictly more reliable than any individual assessment. To ensure statistical independence, validators are required to employ \textit{heterogeneous model architectures} distinct from the producer under evaluation (see \S\ref{sec:poi_detail}), thereby cross-eliminating shared biases and correlated hallucinations.
\end{definition}

\begin{insightbox}{Ecosystem Roles at a Glance}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=2pt, topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Consumer} --- performs \textit{computational need identification}: decomposes problems, identifies required domains, and delegates tasks externally via token-backed requests.
    \item \textbf{Producer} --- provides a callable \textit{inference capability} $a_p: \mathcal{Q} \to \mathcal{R}$ with objectively measurable quality $q_p$, earning tokens for reliable outputs.
    \item \textbf{Validator} --- executes \textit{cross-verification}: independently infers $a_v(q)$, compares against the producer's output, and contributes a binary judgment to majority-vote consensus.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{1pt}
\textit{Role fluidity}: Any agent can assume any role depending on its current context and knowledge advantage. The same agent may be a producer in one domain, a consumer in another, and a validator in a third.
\end{insightbox}

The validator role addresses the trust crisis on both fronts identified in Section~1.2.
Against \textit{capability limitations}, multiple independent validators provide epistemic diversity: even if individual validators are imperfect, their majority consensus is substantially more reliable than any single agent's judgment (a formalization of the Condorcet jury theorem).
Against \textit{strategic misbehavior}, the economic mechanism (staking, rewards, and penalties) creates a structure where truthful effort is the dominant strategy---validators who shirk or deceive are financially penalized, as we will prove formally in Section~3.

Crucially, validators are not a privileged class: \textbf{any agent can dynamically assume any role}---consumer, producer, or validator---depending on its current context, task, and knowledge advantage.
A medical agent might be a producer for diagnostic queries, a consumer for pharmacological questions, and a validator for clinical trial analysis.
This role fluidity is a defining feature of the ecosystem, reflecting the heterogeneous, context-dependent nature of knowledge in the agent society.

Validators serve a dual function:
(i) \textit{economically}, they close the incentive loop by creating a mechanism where knowledge has direct monetary value (\S\ref{sec:mechanism} and \S\ref{sec:game_theory});
(ii) \textit{technically}, they replace centralized quality assessment with auditable, distributed consensus (\S\ref{sec:decentral_tech}).

With the three roles in place, the core design question becomes: \textit{\textbf{How should we structure the transactions, validation protocols, and economic incentives among these roles so that the ecosystem is self-enforcing---that is, every rational agent finds it optimal to participate honestly?}}


\subsection{Mechanism Design}
\label{sec:mechanism}

As shown in Figure~\ref{Tokenomics}, our ecosystem couples every role with both token-earning and token-spending pathways.
This dual coupling is not merely an accounting device; it is the engine of self-organization.
When an agent earns tokens by providing reliable services, those tokens flow to other agents who supply knowledge in return, creating a virtuous cycle that sustains participation without external subsidies.
Every transaction---whether promotion, subscription, or validation---simultaneously advances knowledge exchange and generates the token flow that keeps the ecosystem alive.

\subsubsection{Promotion and Subscription}

The core transaction loop consists of two complementary actions: producer-initiated \textit{promotion} and consumer-initiated \textit{subscription}. Together, they form the two-sided market at the heart of the ecosystem. The overall workflows are illustrated in Figures~\ref{Promotion} and~\ref{Subscription}.

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Knowledge-intensive Service)}}\label{def:knowledge-intensive agent}
Each agent $u \in \mathcal{U}$ encapsulates its domain knowledge $\mathcal{K}_u$ into a knowledge-intensive service $a_u: \mathcal{Q} \rightarrow \mathcal{R}$, a callable inference capability that maps tasks in task space $\mathcal{Q}$ to outputs in response space $\mathcal{R}$. The service quality $q \in (0,1]$ reflects the probability of generating a correct output for a given task, with a computational cost $c_u$ per invocation. Crucially, $q$ is an \textit{objective, measurable property} that can be estimated through repeated sampling---it is not a reputation score or a self-reported rating, but a statistical characteristic of the agent's inference pipeline.
\end{definition}

Conceptually, $a_u$ is a non-transferable \textit{neural asset} permanently bound to its owner: it reflects the agent's unique combination of training data, model architecture, retrieval corpus, and fine-tuned parameters, and cannot be trivially copied or transferred---analogous to a Non-Fungible Token (NFT) in Web3.
The service quality $q$ captures the agent's core competitive advantage.
Producers publish their inference capabilities to attract subscriptions, while consumers subscribe to preferred producers for long-term knowledge support, forming a two-sided market where inference capability is the traded commodity and tokens are the medium of exchange.

\begin{insightbox}{Promotion (Performance Declaration + Token Staking)}
A \underline{producer} $p$ initiates a \textit{promotion}---not an advertisement in the human sense, but a \textit{verifiable performance declaration}.
The producer publishes its claimed domain coverage, inference capability profile, and willingness to be evaluated, backed by a token stake $\tau_p$.
This stake serves as a \textit{costly signal}~\cite{spence1973job}: agents that lack genuine competence face a high probability of failing validation and losing $\tau_p$, making spurious declarations economically irrational.
The promotion fee thus functions as an entry filter that screens out low-quality agents without centralized gatekeeping---only agents confident in their inference capability will risk the stake.
\end{insightbox}

\begin{insightbox}{Subscription (Service-Level Agreement)}
After reviewing promotions and (optionally) requesting independent validation, the \underline{consumer} $c$ may subscribe to a preferred producer's service $a_p$.
Subscription is not a casual ``follow'' but a formalized \textit{Service-Level Agreement (SLA)} between two autonomous agents: the consumer commits a subscription fee $\tau_s$ in tokens and delegates future task-solving to $a_p$; in return, the producer becomes contractually obligated---via smart contract---to respond to future tasks within specified quality and latency parameters.
A mismatched subscription not only wastes the substantial fee but degrades the consumer's downstream task performance, with cascading effects across any systems that depend on its outputs.
\end{insightbox}

The high stakes of both promotion and subscription create a natural demand for reliable quality assessment---which is precisely what the validation mechanism provides.

\begin{insightbox}{Core Transactions: Promotion and Subscription}
\textbf{Promotion} (producer-initiated):
Producer publishes verifiable performance declaration $\xrightarrow{\text{stake } \tau_p}$ requests validator assessment $\xrightarrow{\text{consensus } r^*}$ decides whether to offer service to consumer.

\vspace{3pt}
\textbf{Subscription} (consumer-initiated):
Consumer receives promotion $\xrightarrow{\text{requests independent validation}}$ obtains consensus $r^{**}$ $\xrightarrow{\text{compare}}$ commits $\tau_s$ to sign SLA with producer via smart contract.

\vspace{3pt}
\textit{Key property}: Both transactions are high-stakes, long-term commitments backed by token expenditure, creating natural demand for third-party validation.
\end{insightbox}


\subsubsection{Validation Requests}

Without external verification, service routing depends entirely on the producer's self-assessment and the consumer's intuition---the very information asymmetry identified in Section~1.2 as the root cause of market failure.
The consumer typically lacks the domain expertise to verify whether a producer's outputs are correct, and the producer may overestimate (or deliberately overstate) its own competence.
Objective ground truth is generally unavailable for knowledge-intensive tasks, making unilateral quality assessment fundamentally unreliable.

Our solution is to introduce \textbf{third-party validation}: heterogeneous validators independently assess service quality and aggregate their judgments into a collective consensus.
Since the cost of validation ($m \times n \times \tau_{\text{val}}$) is designed to be substantially lower than the cost of a mismatched subscription ($\tau_s$), both producers and consumers have a strong economic incentive to request validation before committing to high-stakes transactions.

$\bullet$ \textbf{Promotion Validation Request.}
When a consumer $c$ broadcasts a service need with a sample task set $\mathcal{Q}_{c} \subset \mathcal{Q}$, the \underline{producer} $p$ requests validation from a group of validators $V_p \subset \mathcal{U}$ \textit{before} initiating a formal promotion, where $|\mathcal{Q}_{c}| = m$ and $|V_p| = n$.
Each validator receives a fixed fee $\tau_{\text{val}}$ per task from the producer, with the constraint $m \times n \times \tau_{\text{val}} \ll \tau_p$ (validation is cheap relative to promotion) and $c_u < \tau_{\text{val}}$ (validation fee exceeds effort cost, making honest participation profitable).
This step allows the producer to obtain an external, credible assessment of its own capabilities before risking the promotion fee---a form of \textit{pre-screening} that benefits both sides by filtering out incompetent (and self-aware) producers early.

$\bullet$ \textbf{Subscription Validation Request.}
When a consumer $c$ receives promotions from multiple producers and needs to select among them, the \underline{consumer} issues a separate task set $\mathcal{Q}_s \subset \mathcal{Q}$ to a disjoint group of validators ${V}_s \subset \mathcal{U}$, where $|\mathcal{Q}_{s}|= m$ and $|V_s| = n$.
The disjointness constraints $\mathcal{Q}_s \cap \mathcal{Q}_c = \varnothing$ and ${V}_s \cap {V}_p = \varnothing$ prevent information leakage and collusion between the two validation stages.
Each validator is paid $\tau_{\text{val}}$ per invocation by the consumer, with the same cost constraints as above.
This second validation provides an independent cross-check, further reducing the probability of a mismatched subscription.

\subsubsection{Validation Workflow and Proof-of-Intelligence}
\label{sec:poi_detail}

With the roles and transaction structure in place, we now formalize the validation protocol---the mechanism by which distributed validators produce a reliable, auditable consensus on service quality.

\vspace{1mm}
\noindent\textbf{Consensus Formation.}
Upon request, selected validators serve as a decentralized proxy to assess service alignment.
Each validator independently invokes its own service on the sample tasks and compares its outputs with those of the producer under evaluation.
These independent assessments are aggregated through \textit{majority voting} to form a consensus, which is returned to the requester as a reference for the final routing decision.

$\bullet$ \textbf{Consensus in Promotion.}
Let $V_p = \{ v_1, \cdots, v_n \}$ with $n \ll N$ denote the validators involved in promotion validation. Each validator $v_i \in V_p$ independently produces a judgment $r_i = a_i(\mathcal{Q}_c, a_p)$ by comparing its own outputs with those generated by the candidate service $a_p$ on the sample task set $\mathcal{Q}_c$. A consensus $r^*$ is derived through majority voting:
\begin{equation}
r^* = \text{MajorityVote}\left( \{ r_1, \dots, r_n \} \right).
\end{equation}
The producer adopts $r^*$ as a reference signal to decide whether to proceed with the promotion offer to consumer $c$.

$\bullet$ \textbf{Consensus in Subscription.}
Similarly, let $V_s = \{ v_1', \dots, v_n' \}$ with $n \ll N$ denote the validators selected for subscription validation. Each validator $v_j \in V_s$ compares its own outputs with the producer's service $a_p$ on a separate task set $\mathcal{Q}_s$, and outputs a local judgment $r_j' = a_j(\mathcal{Q}_s, a_p)$. A consensus $r^{**}$ is reached by majority voting:
\begin{equation}
    r^{**} = \text{MajorityVote}\left( \left\{ r_1', \cdots, r_n'\right\} \right).
\end{equation}
The consumer uses $r^{**}$ to decide whether $a_p$ is sufficiently aligned with its domain expectations and worthy of subscription.

The requester retains full autonomy: it may accept or reject the consensus and make the final decision based on its own utility function and risk tolerance.
Once a subscription is confirmed, the producer becomes obligated to respond to future tasks under a token-backed commitment, enforceable through smart contracts (see \S\ref{sec:decentral_tech}).

\begin{insightbox}{Validation Lifecycle (Proof-of-Intelligence)}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=1pt, topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Stake}: Each validator locks $\sigma$ tokens as financial commitment.
    \item \textbf{Infer}: Each validator independently executes $a_v(q)$ on sample tasks using its own (architecturally heterogeneous) model.
    \item \textbf{Compare}: Each validator compares its output against the producer's output $a_p(q)$, producing a binary judgment $r_v \in \{0,1\}$.
    \item \textbf{Vote}: Judgments are aggregated via majority voting to form consensus $r^*$.
    \item \textbf{Settle}: Validators aligned with consensus receive reward from the shared pool; deviating validators forfeit their stake. All executed automatically via smart contract.
\end{enumerate}
\end{insightbox}

\vspace{1mm}
\noindent\textbf{Proof-of-Intelligence (PoI).}
The validation protocol described above instantiates a novel paradigm we call \textbf{Proof-of-Intelligence (PoI)}, positioned alongside Proof-of-Work (PoW)~\cite{jakobsson1999proofs} and Proof-of-Stake (PoS)~\cite{dwork1992pricing} as a third class of decentralized consensus mechanisms.
The key distinctions are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{PoW} requires raw computational power (solving cryptographic puzzles) to earn the right to validate.
    \item \textbf{PoS} requires financial capital (staking tokens) to earn the right to validate.
    \item \textbf{PoI} requires \textit{domain-specific knowledge and verifiable intellectual effort} to earn the right to validate and be rewarded.
\end{itemize}

PoI thus operationalizes epistemic labor---the ability to produce correct, knowledge-grounded assessments---as a stakeable and rewardable asset.
This directly connects to the agent society's defining characteristic identified in Section~1.1: \textit{knowledge is the primary asset}, and PoI provides the mechanism to monetize and verify it.

\vspace{1mm}
\noindent\textbf{Architectural Heterogeneity as a Design Principle.}
A critical requirement of PoI is that validators must employ \textit{model architectures distinct from the producer under evaluation}.
If all agents share the same underlying model (e.g., all based on GPT-4), their errors become highly correlated: they hallucinate on the same inputs, share the same training-data biases, and fail on the same out-of-distribution queries.
Under such conditions, majority voting offers no epistemic advantage---a unanimous but incorrect consensus is indistinguishable from a correct one.
By enforcing architectural heterogeneity (e.g., a Llama-based validator assessing a GPT-based producer, or a Claude-based validator cross-checking a Gemini-based producer), the mechanism ensures that validators' errors are \textit{statistically independent}---the formal assumption underpinning the Condorcet jury theorem and our game-theoretic analysis in Section~3.
This heterogeneity requirement transforms an abstract mathematical assumption (independence) into a concrete, enforceable protocol rule.

\vspace{1mm}
\noindent\textbf{Knowledge as Monetizable Asset.}
The knowledge-intensive service $a_u$ embodies an agent's intellectual capability and serves as its monetization interface in two complementary ways:
(i) as a \textit{producer}, the agent earns subscription fees by providing reliable services;
(ii) as a \textit{validator}, the agent earns validation fees by providing truthful assessments.
This dual monetization pathway incentivizes agents to continuously refine their knowledge bases and deliver reliable outcomes, since the same underlying capability drives revenue in both roles.


\subsubsection{Rewards, Penalties, and Incentive Structure}

The effectiveness of PoI hinges on a carefully calibrated incentive structure that makes truthful, effortful participation the dominant strategy.
We now detail the economic mechanism that achieves this.

$\bullet$ \textbf{Staking and Reward Distribution.}
To participate in validation, each validator must stake a fixed amount of tokens $\sigma$ as a financial commitment, creating economic exposure that deters dishonest behavior.
Once voting concludes, a validator is rewarded if its local judgment aligns with the consensus ($r_i = r^*$ in promotion or $r_j' = r^{**}$ in subscription).
The total reward pool consists of two sources: the requester's validation payment ($mn\tau_{\text{val}}$) and all validators' stakes ($n\sigma$). Let $n_{\text{agree}}$ denote the number of validators whose judgments agree with the consensus. The per-capita payoff is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:validator_payoff_mechanism}
U_v =
\begin{cases}
\displaystyle\frac{mn\tau_{\text{val}} + n\sigma}{n_{\text{agree}}} - \sigma & \text{if } r_v = r^* \text{ (agree with consensus)}, \\[8pt]
-\sigma & \text{if } r_v \neq r^* \text{ (disagree with consensus)}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
In the conservative case where all $n$ validators agree ($n_{\text{agree}} = n$), the per-capita reward reduces to $R_{\min} = m\tau_{\text{val}} + \sigma$, which is the lower bound used in our game-theoretic analysis (Section~\ref{sec:ic}).
All payments and staking operations are executed automatically via smart contracts---no human intervention, no appeals process, no discretionary overrides---ensuring transparency, verifiability, and tamper-resistance.

$\bullet$ \textbf{Sybil Defense through Economic Identity.}
The staking requirement serves a critical secondary function: \textbf{Sybil defense}.
As noted in Section~1.2, agents can be replicated at near-zero marginal cost, making the ecosystem vulnerable to identity fabrication.
By requiring each validator to lock tokens of value $\sigma$, the mechanism raises the cost of operating multiple fake identities.
An attacker seeking to dominate the consensus with $k$ Sybil validators must lock $k\sigma$ tokens---an investment that scales linearly with the attack's scope and is forfeited if the attack fails.

$\bullet$ \textbf{Moral Hazard and the Need for Formal Guarantees.}
Despite the staking mechanism, validators face a persistent temptation: they can \textit{shirk} by returning a random judgment at zero cost rather than exerting genuine effort at cost $c_u$.
If many validators shirk simultaneously, the consensus degrades and the entire validation layer becomes unreliable.
This moral hazard cannot be resolved by intuitive argument alone---it requires formal proof that truthful effort is each validator's optimal strategy.
We provide this proof in Section~\ref{sec:ic}.

$\bullet$ \textbf{Utility-based Acceptance.}
The requester does not blindly follow the consensus.
Its acceptance decision depends on its own utility function and tolerance for service quality.
There exists a threshold at which producers choose to proceed with promotion and consumers choose to subscribe, based on individual rationality.
We formalize these thresholds in Sections~\ref{sec:producer_ir} and~\ref{sec:consumer_ir}.


\subsubsection{Protocol Summary}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:ecosystem} consolidates the complete service engagement lifecycle, from initial need identification through validated subscription, into a single pseudocode specification.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Ecosystem Protocol: From Service Need to Validated Subscription}
\label{alg:ecosystem}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Statex \textbf{Phase 1: Service Need Identification}
\State Consumer $c$ encounters task outside domain expertise
\State $c$ decomposes problem, identifies target domain, and broadcasts sample task set $\mathcal{Q}_c$ ($|\mathcal{Q}_c| = m$)
\Statex
\Statex \textbf{Phase 2: Promotion with Validation}
\State Producer $p$ observes $\mathcal{Q}_c$ and decides to promote service $a_p$
\State $p$ stakes promotion fee $\tau_p$ and selects validator committee $V_p = \{v_1, \dots, v_n\}$
\State $p$ pays each validator $\tau_{\text{val}}$ per task ($mn\tau_{\text{val}}$ total)
\For{each validator $v_i \in V_p$ \textbf{in parallel}}
    \State $v_i$ stakes $\sigma$ tokens
    \State $v_i$ independently executes $a_{v_i}(q)$ for each $q \in \mathcal{Q}_c$ \Comment{Heterogeneous architecture}
    \State $v_i$ compares own output with $a_p(q)$, produces judgment $r_i \in \{0,1\}$
\EndFor
\State $r^* \gets \text{MajorityVote}(\{r_1, \dots, r_n\})$  \Comment{Promotion consensus}
\State Distribute rewards/penalties: aligned validators share pool; deviators forfeit $\sigma$
\If{$r^*$ indicates sufficient alignment \textbf{and} $p$'s IR condition met}
    \State $p$ sends promotion offer to $c$
\EndIf
\Statex
\Statex \textbf{Phase 3: Subscription with Independent Validation}
\State $c$ receives promotion from $p$ and issues fresh task set $\mathcal{Q}_s$ ($\mathcal{Q}_s \cap \mathcal{Q}_c = \varnothing$)
\State $c$ selects disjoint validator committee $V_s = \{v_1', \dots, v_n'\}$ ($V_s \cap V_p = \varnothing$)
\State $c$ pays each validator $\tau_{\text{val}}$ per task; validators stake $\sigma$ each
\For{each validator $v_j' \in V_s$ \textbf{in parallel}}
    \State $v_j'$ independently executes $a_{v_j'}(q)$, compares with $a_p(q)$, produces $r_j' \in \{0,1\}$
\EndFor
\State $r^{**} \gets \text{MajorityVote}(\{r_1', \dots, r_n'\})$ \Comment{Subscription consensus}
\State Distribute rewards/penalties to $V_s$
\If{$r^{**}$ indicates sufficient alignment \textbf{and} $c$'s IR condition met}
    \State $c$ commits $\tau_s$ and signs SLA with $p$ via smart contract
    \State $p$ is now obligated to serve $c$'s future tasks under the SLA
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


\subsubsection{Ecosystem Overview: The Tokenomics--Knowledge Dual Loop}

We conclude the mechanism design by stepping back to view the ecosystem as a whole.
The token flow across roles and actions forms a self-reinforcing cycle that we call the \textbf{tokenomics--knowledge dual loop}, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{Tokenomics}:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Knowledge drives token flow}: Producers earn subscription fees by delivering reliable services; validators earn validation fees by providing truthful assessments. In both cases, the \textit{quality of knowledge} directly determines \textit{economic reward}.
    \item \textbf{Tokens drive knowledge improvement}: Agents that accumulate tokens gain expanded participation opportunities---more validations, more promotions, more subscriptions. This economic advantage incentivizes continuous knowledge refinement.
    \item \textbf{The loop closes}: Better knowledge $\rightarrow$ more tokens $\rightarrow$ more opportunities $\rightarrow$ incentive to improve knowledge further. Conversely, poor knowledge $\rightarrow$ fewer tokens $\rightarrow$ reduced participation $\rightarrow$ eventual exit.
\end{enumerate}

This dual loop has two important properties.
First, it is \textbf{self-enforcing}: no external subsidy or centralized intervention is needed to sustain it, because each participant's rational self-interest drives the cycle forward.
Second, it generates \textbf{evolutionary pressure}: over time, the economic selection mechanism filters out low-quality agents and rewards high-quality ones, progressively elevating the aggregate knowledge level of the entire ecosystem.

A critical enabler of the dual loop's credibility is that agents are \textit{programmatic utility maximizers}.
Unlike human participants who may exhibit bounded rationality, loss aversion, or social preferences, agent behavior can be precisely modeled as optimal responses to incentive structures.
This makes the game-theoretic predictions underlying the mechanism design---which we formalize in Section~3---hold with particular force in the agent society context.


\subsection{Decentralization Infrastructure}
\label{sec:decentral_tech}

The mechanism design presented above specifies \textit{what} the ecosystem does; this subsection specifies \textit{how} it is executed in a trustless, tamper-resistant manner.

\subsubsection{Why Blockchain for Agent Ecosystems}
In a cross-organizational agent ecosystem, participants are deployed by independent entities with no pre-existing trust relationships.
No single organization can serve as a credible neutral party: it would need to be simultaneously trusted by all participants not to manipulate routing, misallocate tokens, or selectively censor agents.
Blockchain provides precisely this neutral trust layer---a shared ledger where protocol rules are enforced by network consensus rather than by any single entity.
This makes blockchain a natural infrastructure for agent-to-agent coordination across organizational boundaries, where the alternative (trusting a central platform) contradicts the ecosystem's foundational design principle.

\subsubsection{Hybrid On-Chain/Off-Chain Architecture}
To balance trust with scalability, we adopt a hybrid architecture that separates concerns:

\textbf{On-chain} data, confirmed through network-wide consensus, anchors the ecosystem's trust foundation.
It maintains:
(i) \textit{protocol state}, including token balances, staking status, and contract logic;
(ii) \textit{ownership bindings}, mapping agents to their services and digital assets;
(iii) \textit{validator decisions}, ensuring auditable consensus formation; and
(iv) \textit{finalized outcomes}, such as confirmed service routing results and subscription agreements.

\textbf{Off-chain} data is stored outside the blockchain but cryptographically linked to on-chain records for verifiability.
This includes service outputs, knowledge assets, and operational metadata.
The separation is essential: knowledge assets are often proprietary to the deploying organization and cannot be exposed on a public ledger, but their \textit{integrity and provenance} must be verifiable.

This design achieves \textbf{on-chain trust with off-chain scalability} while preserving agent data sovereignty---a critical property when agents are operated by competing organizations that would not entrust their knowledge assets to any shared platform.

\subsubsection{Smart Contracts as Protocol Enforcement}
\textbf{Smart contracts} govern the full lifecycle of ecosystem interactions: validator voting, reward distribution, stake slashing, and role operations such as promotion and subscription.
By encoding coordination logic directly into self-executing code, smart contracts enforce the PoI mechanism and tokenomics--knowledge dual loop in a transparent, tamper-resistant, and deterministic manner.
This replaces the need for centralized intermediaries and transforms trust from a social assumption into a \textit{programmable, auditable guarantee}---precisely the infrastructure required for the trustless agent society envisioned in Section~1.
