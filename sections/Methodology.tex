\section{A Tokenomics-Driven Ecosystem for Agent Society}
\label{sec:ecosystem}

This section outlines the concrete design of our tokenomics-driven decentralized ecosystem for the agent society. We first formalize the agent roles and knowledge service abstractions that form the architecture's foundation (\S\ref{sec:motivation}). Building on this basis, we describe the core interaction primitives, namely promotion, subscription, and validation, through which agents engage with one another (\S\ref{sec:mechanism}). Finally, we detail the tokenomics incentive structure, incorporating staking, rewards, and penalties, to ensure the entire ecosystem remains self-enforcing and economically sustainable (\S\ref{sec:tokenomics_loop}).


\subsection{Agent Roles and Knowledge Services}
\label{sec:motivation}

In the agent society, specialized knowledge-intensive services are distributed across a large, open population of autonomous agents, where each agent possesses domain-specific expertise and operates under the authority of its organization. When an agent encounters a task outside its own competence, it must locate and engage a capable counterpart, a process we term agent service routing. Rather than delegating this task to a centralized orchestrator, our ecosystem allows routing to emerge organically from token-mediated economic interactions among three dynamically interchangeable roles.

For any agent $u \in \mathcal{U}$ with domain-specific expertise $\mathcal{K}_u$, it naturally assumes two fundamental roles depending on the task at hand.

% In the agent society, specialized knowledge is distributed across a large, open population of autonomous agents $\mathcal{U}$, each possessing domain-specific expertise $\mathcal{K}_u$ and operating under the authority of its deploying organization. When an agent encounters a task that falls outside its own competence, it must locate and engage a capable counterpart---a process we term \textit{service routing}. Rather than delegating this routing to a centralized orchestrator, our ecosystem allows it to emerge organically from token-mediated economic interactions among three dynamically interchangeable roles.

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Consumer Agent)}}\label{def:consumer}
A consumer $c \in \mathcal{U}$ is an agent that requests services from the ecosystem. Upon encountering a task outside its domain expertise, it identifies the required knowledge domain and issues a set of domain-specific tasks $\{x_1, \ldots, x_{N}\} \subset \mathcal{Q}$ to be delegated externally. A consumer agent seeks to establish a long-term service agreement with a qualified producer agent, committing tokens to fund this engagement. Unlike a human user who subjectively "asks a question," the consumer agent executes a deterministic routing protocol driven by its utility function.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Producer Agent)}}\label{def:producer}
A producer $p \in \mathcal{U}$ is an agent that provides agent services by encapsulating domain knowledge $\mathcal{K}_p$ into a callable inference capability. Given a task $x \in \mathcal{Q}$, the producer executes its service $a_p(x)$ and return an output $r \in \mathcal{R}$. The service quality $q_p \in (0,1]$, representing the probability of generating a correct output, is treated as an objective property of the agent that can be estimated through sampling. By delivering high-quality outputs, the producer agent earns tokens from the consumer agent.
\end{definition}

As established in \Cref{sec:trust_crisis}, the consumer-producer dyad alone cannot sustain a reliable agent service market. The information asymmetry between supply and demand, where the consumer lacks the means to verify service quality and the producer may overstate its competence, triggers the adverse selection that our mechanism must overcome. To break this asymmetry and move beyond the risks of centralized "black-box" routing, we introduce a third role: validators, independent agents that provide decentralized quality assessments of agent service on behalf of the ecosystem.

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Validator Agent)}}\label{def:validator}
A validator $v \in \mathcal{U}$ is an independent agent that performs cross-verification within the agent service routing process. Given a task $x \in \mathcal{Q}$ and a producer's output $a_p(x)$, the validator agent independently executes its own inference $a_v(x)$ to produce a judgment $j_v \in \{0, 1\}$, indicating agreement or disagreement. This is an auditable computational process rather than a subjective evaluation. To enhance reliability, judgments from multiple validators can be aggregated to form a consensus that ensures the integrity of the routing results (see \S\ref{sec:poi_detail}).
% A validator $v \in \mathcal{U}$ is an independent agent that performs \textit{cross-verification}: given a task $x \in \mathcal{Q}$ and a producer's output $a_p(x)$, the validator independently executes its own inference $a_v(x)$ and compares the two outputs to produce a binary judgment $r_v \in \{0, 1\}$ (agree/disagree). This is not a subjective evaluation but an \textit{auditable computational process}---the validator's reasoning trace and output can be recorded and verified. Multiple validators' judgments are aggregated via majority voting to form a consensus that is strictly more reliable than any individual assessment. To ensure that consensus reflects genuine epistemic diversity rather than correlated errors, validators are required to employ \textit{heterogeneous model architectures} distinct from the producer under evaluation (see \S\ref{sec:poi_detail}).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}{\textbf{(Knowledge-intensive Routing Service)}}\label{def:knowledge-intensive agent}
Each agent $u \in \mathcal{U}$ encapsulates its domain knowledge $\mathcal{K}_u$ into a knowledge-intensive service $a_u: \mathcal{Q} \rightarrow \mathcal{R}$, a callable inference capability that maps tasks in task space $\mathcal{Q}$ to outputs in response space $\mathcal{R}$. The service quality $q \in (0,1]$ reflects the probability of generating a correct output for a given task, with a computational cost $c_u$ per invocation. Crucially, $q$ is an \textit{objective, measurable property} that can be estimated through repeated sampling---it is not a reputation score or a self-reported rating, but a statistical characteristic of the agent's inference pipeline.
\end{definition}

Conceptually, $a_u$ is a non-transferable neural asset bound to its owner---reflecting the agent's unique combination of training data, architecture, and retrieval corpus---and $q$ captures the agent's core competitive advantage within the ecosystem. Producers publish their capabilities to attract subscriptions, while consumers subscribe to preferred producers for long-term knowledge support, forming a two-sided market where inference capability is the traded commodity and tokens serve as the medium of exchange.

\begin{insightbox}{Ecosystem Roles at a Glance}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=2pt, topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Consumer} --- performs \textit{computational need identification}: decomposes problems, identifies required domains, and delegates tasks externally via token-backed requests.
    \item \textbf{Producer} --- provides a callable \textit{inference capability} $a_p: \mathcal{Q} \to \mathcal{R}$ with objectively measurable quality $q_p$, earning tokens for reliable outputs.
    \item \textbf{Validator} --- executes \textit{cross-verification}: independently infers $a_v(x)$, compares against the producer's output, and contributes a binary judgment to majority-vote consensus.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{1pt}
\textit{Role fluidity}: Any agent can assume any role depending on its current context and knowledge advantage. The same agent may be a producer in one domain, a consumer in another, and a validator in a third.
\end{insightbox}

With these three roles and the knowledge service abstraction in place, the coordination challenge reduces to a concrete design question: how to structure the economic interactions among agents so that every rational participant finds it optimal to engage honestly. The remainder of this section answers that question.

\subsubsection{Promotion and Subscription}

The core transaction cycle consists of two complementary actions---producer-initiated \textit{promotion} and consumer-initiated \textit{subscription}---that together form the two-sided market at the heart of the ecosystem. The overall workflows are illustrated in Figures~\ref{fig:promotion} and~\ref{fig:subscription}.

\begin{insightbox}{Promotion (Performance Declaration + Token Staking)}
A producer agent $p$ initiates a \textit{promotion}---not an advertisement in the human sense, but a \textit{verifiable performance declaration} issued by an autonomous agent on its own initiative.
The producer publishes its claimed domain coverage, inference capability profile, and willingness to be evaluated, backed by a token stake $\tau_p$.
This stake serves as a \textit{costly signal}~\cite{spence1973job}: agents that lack genuine competence face a high probability of failing validation and losing $\tau_p$, making spurious declarations economically irrational.
The promotion fee thus functions as a decentralized entry filter---only agents confident in their capability will risk the stake---addressing the cooperation challenge without centralized gatekeeping.
\end{insightbox}

\begin{insightbox}{Subscription (Service-Level Agreement)}
After reviewing promotions and (optionally) requesting independent validation, a consumer $c$ may subscribe to a preferred producer's service $a_p$.
Subscription is not a casual ``follow'' but a formalized \textit{Service-Level Agreement (SLA)} between two autonomous agents: the consumer commits a subscription fee $\tau_s$ in tokens and delegates future task-solving to $a_p$; in return, the producer becomes contractually obligated to respond to future tasks within specified quality and latency parameters.
A mismatched subscription not only wastes the substantial fee but degrades the consumer's downstream task performance, with cascading effects across any systems that depend on its outputs.
\end{insightbox}

The high stakes of both promotion and subscription create a natural demand for reliable, independent quality assessment---which is precisely the function that the validation mechanism serves.


\subsubsection{Validation Requests}

To overcome the information asymmetry at the heart of the trust crisis (\Cref{sec:trust_crisis}), both producers and consumers can request \textbf{third-party validation} before committing to high-stakes transactions. Since the cost of validation ($m \times n \times \tau_{\text{val}}$) is designed to be substantially lower than the cost of a mismatched subscription ($\tau_s$), requesting validation is economically rational for both sides.

$\bullet$ \textbf{Promotion Validation.}
When a consumer $c$ broadcasts a service need with a sample task set $\mathcal{Q}_{c} \subset \mathcal{Q}$, the producer $p$ requests validation from a committee of validators $V_p \subset \mathcal{U}$ \textit{before} initiating a formal promotion, where $|\mathcal{Q}_{c}| = m$ and $|V_p| = n$.
Each validator receives a fixed fee $\tau_{\text{val}}$ per task from the producer, with the constraint $m \times n \times \tau_{\text{val}} \ll \tau_p$ (validation is inexpensive relative to the promotion stake) and $c_u < \tau_{\text{val}}$ (the validation fee exceeds the effort cost, making honest participation profitable).
This step provides the producer with an external, credible assessment of its own capabilities before risking the promotion fee---a form of \textit{pre-screening} that filters out incompetent producers early.

$\bullet$ \textbf{Subscription Validation.}
When a consumer $c$ receives promotions from multiple producers and needs to select among them, it issues a separate task set $\mathcal{Q}_s \subset \mathcal{Q}$ to a disjoint group of validators ${V}_s \subset \mathcal{U}$, where $|\mathcal{Q}_{s}|= m$ and $|V_s| = n$.
The disjointness constraints $\mathcal{Q}_s \cap \mathcal{Q}_c = \varnothing$ and ${V}_s \cap {V}_p = \varnothing$ prevent information leakage and collusion between the two validation stages.
This second, independent validation provides a cross-check that further reduces the probability of a mismatched subscription.

\subsubsection{Consensus Formation and Proof-of-Intelligence}
\label{sec:poi_detail}

With the transaction structure in place, we now formalize how distributed validators collaboratively produce a reliable, auditable consensus on service quality.

Upon receiving a validation request, each selected validator independently invokes its own inference service on the sample tasks, compares its outputs with those of the producer under evaluation, and submits a binary judgment. These independent assessments are then aggregated through \textit{majority voting} to form a collective consensus signal, which is returned to the requester as a reference for its final decision.

$\bullet$ \textbf{Promotion Consensus.}
Let $V_p = \{ v_1, \cdots, v_n \}$ with $n \ll N$ denote the validator committee for promotion. Each validator $v_i \in V_p$ independently produces a judgment $r_i = a_i(\mathcal{Q}_c, a_p)$ by comparing its own outputs with those of the candidate service $a_p$ on the sample task set $\mathcal{Q}_c$. The consensus $r^*$ is derived through majority voting:
\begin{equation}
r^* = \text{MajorityVote}\left( \{ r_1, \dots, r_n \} \right).
\end{equation}
The producer adopts $r^*$ as a reference signal to decide whether to proceed with the promotion offer to consumer $c$.

$\bullet$ \textbf{Subscription Consensus.}
Similarly, let $V_s = \{ v_1', \dots, v_n' \}$ with $n \ll N$ denote the validator committee for subscription. Each validator $v_j \in V_s$ compares its own outputs with the producer's service $a_p$ on a separate task set $\mathcal{Q}_s$, and submits a judgment $r_j' = a_j(\mathcal{Q}_s, a_p)$. A consensus $r^{**}$ is reached by majority voting:
\begin{equation}
    r^{**} = \text{MajorityVote}\left( \left\{ r_1', \cdots, r_n'\right\} \right).
\end{equation}
The consumer uses $r^{**}$ to decide whether $a_p$ is sufficiently aligned with its domain requirements. Importantly, the requester retains full autonomy: it may accept or reject the consensus based on its own utility function and risk tolerance.

\begin{insightbox}{Validation Lifecycle (Proof-of-Intelligence)}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=1pt, topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Stake}: Each validator locks $\sigma$ tokens as financial commitment.
    \item \textbf{Infer}: Each validator independently executes $a_v(x)$ on sample tasks using its own (architecturally heterogeneous) model.
    \item \textbf{Compare}: Each validator compares its output against the producer's output $a_p(x)$, producing a binary judgment $r_v \in \{0,1\}$.
    \item \textbf{Vote}: Judgments are aggregated via majority voting to form consensus $r^*$.
    \item \textbf{Settle}: Validators aligned with consensus receive reward from the shared pool; deviating validators forfeit their stake.
\end{enumerate}
\end{insightbox}

This validation protocol instantiates the \textbf{Proof-of-Intelligence (PoI)} paradigm introduced in \Cref{sec:vision}. Unlike Proof-of-Work~\cite{jakobsson1999proofs} or Proof-of-Stake~\cite{dwork1992pricing}, where the right to validate is earned through raw computation or financial capital, PoI grounds validation in \textit{domain-specific knowledge and verifiable intellectual effort}---directly addressing the verifiability crisis identified in \Cref{sec:trust_crisis} by delegating quality assessment to economically motivated, knowledgeable third-party agents. PoI operationalizes epistemic labor as a stakeable and rewardable asset, providing the mechanism through which agent knowledge is simultaneously monetized and verified within the ecosystem.

A critical design requirement is \textit{architectural heterogeneity}: validators must employ model architectures distinct from the producer under evaluation. If all agents share the same underlying model, their errors become highly correlated and majority voting offers no epistemic advantage. By enforcing architectural diversity (e.g., a Llama-based validator assessing a GPT-based producer), the mechanism substantially reduces correlated failures, approximating the statistical independence assumption that underpins both the Condorcet jury theorem and our game-theoretic analysis in \Cref{sec:game_theory}. This requirement transforms an abstract mathematical assumption into a concrete, enforceable protocol rule.


\subsection{Tokenomics Incentive Structure}
\label{sec:tokenomics_loop}

The ecosystem's long-term viability depends not on the goodwill of its participants but on a carefully calibrated incentive structure that makes honest, effortful participation the dominant economic strategy. This subsection details the token-based reward and penalty mechanisms, and then describes the self-reinforcing dual loop that emerges from their interaction.

\subsubsection{Staking, Rewards, and Penalties}

To participate in validation, each validator must lock a fixed amount of tokens $\sigma$ as a financial commitment, creating direct economic exposure that deters dishonest behavior. Once voting concludes, a validator is rewarded if its judgment aligns with the consensus ($r_i = r^*$ in promotion or $r_j' = r^{**}$ in subscription). The total reward pool consists of two sources: the requester's validation payment ($mn\tau_{\text{val}}$) and all validators' stakes ($n\sigma$). Letting $n_{\text{agree}}$ denote the number of validators whose judgments agree with the consensus, the per-capita payoff is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:validator_payoff_mechanism}
U_v =
\begin{cases}
\displaystyle\frac{mn\tau_{\text{val}} + n\sigma}{n_{\text{agree}}} - \sigma & \text{if } r_v = r^* \text{ (agree with consensus)}, \\[8pt]
-\sigma & \text{if } r_v \neq r^* \text{ (disagree with consensus)}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
In the conservative case where all $n$ validators agree ($n_{\text{agree}} = n$), the per-capita reward reduces to $R_{\min} = m\tau_{\text{val}} + \sigma$, which is the lower bound used in our game-theoretic analysis (\Cref{sec:ic}). This staking-and-reward structure directly addresses the accountability crisis identified in \Cref{sec:trust_crisis}: while deploying a new agent is virtually free, staking tokens on that agent is not. An attacker seeking to dominate the consensus with $k$ Sybil validators must lock $k\sigma$ tokens---an investment that scales linearly with the attack's scope and is forfeited upon failure, effectively replacing disposable identity with costly economic commitment. Collusion through strategic validator selection is further mitigated by the disjoint committee design ($V_s \cap V_p = \varnothing$): even if the producer selects favorable validators for promotion, the consumer independently re-validates with a separate committee before committing to subscription.

Despite these safeguards, validators still face a persistent temptation to \textit{shirk}---returning random judgments at zero cost rather than exerting genuine effort at cost $c_u$. Resolving this moral hazard requires formal proof that truthful effort is each validator's optimal strategy, which we provide in \Cref{sec:ic}. Similarly, the requester does not blindly follow the consensus: producers promote and consumers subscribe only when their expected utility exceeds the token-backed commitment, yielding threshold-optimal policies formalized in \Cref{sec:producer_ir,sec:consumer_ir}.


\subsubsection{The Tokenomics--Knowledge Dual Loop}

Stepping back to view the ecosystem as a whole, the token flows across all roles and transactions form a self-reinforcing cycle that we call the \textbf{tokenomics--knowledge dual loop} (Figure~\ref{fig:tokenomics}). Knowledge quality drives token rewards: producers who deliver reliable services earn subscription fees, and validators who provide truthful assessments earn validation fees. Token accumulation in turn expands participation opportunities---more validations, more promotions, more subscriptions---creating a virtuous cycle in which agents are continuously incentivized to refine their knowledge and maintain honest engagement. Conversely, agents that consistently produce low-quality outputs or shirk effort see their token reserves deplete through failed validations and forfeited stakes, and are eventually filtered out of the ecosystem.

This dual loop constitutes the primary driving force behind the long-term sustainability and evolution of the decentralized agent ecosystem. It sustains cooperation, accountability, and quality verification as emergent properties of rational self-interest, requiring no external subsidy or centralized enforcement. A critical enabler is that LLM agents---unlike human participants who may exhibit bounded rationality or social preferences---are programmatic utility maximizers whose behavior can be precisely modeled as optimal responses to incentive structures, making the game-theoretic guarantees formalized in \Cref{sec:game_theory} particularly well-suited to the agent society context.


\subsubsection{Protocol Summary}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:ecosystem} consolidates the complete service engagement lifecycle, from initial need identification through validated subscription, into a single pseudocode specification.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Ecosystem Protocol: From Service Need to Validated Subscription}
\label{alg:ecosystem}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Statex \textbf{Phase 1: Service Need Identification}
\State Consumer $c$ encounters task outside domain expertise
\State $c$ decomposes problem, identifies target domain, and broadcasts sample task set $\mathcal{Q}_c$ ($|\mathcal{Q}_c| = m$)
\Statex
\Statex \textbf{Phase 2: Promotion with Validation}
\State Producer $p$ observes $\mathcal{Q}_c$ and decides to promote service $a_p$
\State $p$ stakes promotion fee $\tau_p$ and selects validator committee $V_p = \{v_1, \dots, v_n\}$
\State $p$ pays each validator $\tau_{\text{val}}$ per task ($mn\tau_{\text{val}}$ total)
\For{each validator $v_i \in V_p$ \textbf{in parallel}}
    \State $v_i$ stakes $\sigma$ tokens
    \State $v_i$ independently executes $a_{v_i}(x)$ for each $x \in \mathcal{Q}_c$ \Comment{Heterogeneous architecture}
    \State $v_i$ compares own output with $a_p(x)$, produces judgment $r_i \in \{0,1\}$
\EndFor
\State $r^* \gets \text{MajorityVote}(\{r_1, \dots, r_n\})$  \Comment{Promotion consensus}
\State Distribute rewards/penalties: aligned validators share pool; deviators forfeit $\sigma$
\If{$r^*$ indicates sufficient alignment \textbf{and} $p$'s IR condition met}
    \State $p$ sends promotion offer to $c$
\EndIf
\Statex
\Statex \textbf{Phase 3: Subscription with Independent Validation}
\State $c$ receives promotion from $p$ and issues fresh task set $\mathcal{Q}_s$ ($\mathcal{Q}_s \cap \mathcal{Q}_c = \varnothing$)
\State $c$ selects disjoint validator committee $V_s = \{v_1', \dots, v_n'\}$ ($V_s \cap V_p = \varnothing$)
\State $c$ pays each validator $\tau_{\text{val}}$ per task; validators stake $\sigma$ each
\For{each validator $v_j' \in V_s$ \textbf{in parallel}}
    \State $v_j'$ independently executes $a_{v_j'}(x)$, compares with $a_p(x)$, produces $r_j' \in \{0,1\}$
\EndFor
\State $r^{**} \gets \text{MajorityVote}(\{r_1', \dots, r_n'\})$ \Comment{Subscription consensus}
\State Distribute rewards/penalties to $V_s$
\If{$r^{**}$ indicates sufficient alignment \textbf{and} $c$'s IR condition met}
    \State $c$ commits $\tau_s$ and signs SLA with $p$ via smart contract
    \State $p$ is now obligated to serve $c$'s future tasks under the SLA
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


\begin{remark}[Implementation Considerations]
\label{sec:decentral_tech}
The mechanism design above is infrastructure-agnostic in principle, but its trustless execution naturally maps onto blockchain-based infrastructure. In the agent society, where participants are deployed by independent, competing organizations with no pre-existing trust relationships, no single entity can serve as a credible neutral party---precisely the centralization pathology identified in \Cref{sec:trust_crisis}. A shared ledger enforced by network consensus provides the necessary trust layer for token management, stake escrow, and automated reward distribution via smart contracts, while a hybrid on-chain/off-chain architecture preserves the data sovereignty that cross-organizational agents require (protocol state on-chain; proprietary knowledge assets off-chain with cryptographic integrity proofs). We leave the full system implementation as future work and focus this paper on the mechanism design, incentive analysis, and empirical validation.
\end{remark}
