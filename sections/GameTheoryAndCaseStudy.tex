\section{Theoretical Foundations and Empirical Validation}

This section provides the game-theoretic foundation for the ecosystem designed in \Cref{sec:ecosystem} (\S\ref{sec:game_theory}) and validates the framework empirically through a case study (\S\ref{sec:case_study}).

\subsection{Game-Theoretic Guarantees}
\label{sec:game_theory}

Our tokenomics-driven ecosystem succeeds only if participation remains individually rational and incentive-compatible across all roles: validators must exert effort, producers must promote selectively, and consumers must subscribe only when evidence justifies the cost. We derive these conditions sequentially, each building on the previous one. \S\ref{sec:setup} defines the Bayesian game and assumptions. \S\ref{sec:ic}--\S\ref{sec:consumer_ir} establish three conditions: validator incentive compatibility (V-IC), producer individual rationality (P-IR), and consumer individual rationality (C-IR). \S\ref{sec:bne} shows that these conditions are mutually consistent, forming a symmetric Bayesian--Nash equilibrium. \S\ref{sec:robustness} verifies that this equilibrium survives realistic departures from the idealized assumptions. We present proof sketches in the main text and defer full derivations to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_proofs}.

\subsubsection{Model Setup and Assumptions}
\label{sec:setup}

We model the ecosystem as a Bayesian game of incomplete information among validator, producer, and consumer agents. Each agent holds a private quality type that determines its task-processing accuracy and service reliability, and all interactions are mediated by token-backed commitments.

\noindent\textbf{Game structure.}
Each service engagement proceeds in four stages:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textit{Nature's Move.} Every agent $u$ is endowed with a private quality type $q_u \in (\tfrac{1}{2}, 1]$ drawn i.i.d.\ from a common prior with density $f$. Each agent observes its own type but not those of others, creating the information asymmetry that necessitates validation.
    \item \textit{Validation Stage.} The requester initiates validation by committing the required token payment. A committee $\mathcal{V}$ of $n$ validators ($n$ being odd) is assembled by the protocol and presented with $m$ sample tasks. Each validator $v_i$ locks a stake $\sigma$, chooses an effort level $e_i \in \{0, 1\}$, and submits a per-task report. Reports are aggregated via majority vote to yield a consensus $Z\in \{0, 1\}$.
    \item \textit{Decision Stage.} The requester observes $Z$ and decides whether to proceed (promote or subscribe).
    \item \textit{Payoff Realization.} Validators whose reports align with the consensus receive a share of the reward pool, while those who disagree forfeit their stake $\sigma$. The requester's token commitments ($\tau_p$ or $\tau_s$) are settled according to the protocol rules.
\end{enumerate}
\noindent As detailed in \Cref{sec:mechanism}, while the ecosystem employs two validation stages (promotion and subscription) with disjoint committees ($V_p \cap V_s = \varnothing$), they share an identical validation substructure. We therefore analyze a single stage without loss of generality.

\noindent\textbf{Strategy spaces.}
An agent's strategy is defined by its role-specific mapping. A validator's strategy maps its quality type $q_v$ to an effort choice $e \in \{0, 1\}$. A producer's strategy maps $q_p$ to a binary promotion decision, made before observing the validation outcome. A consumer's strategy maps the observed consensus signal $Z$ to a binary subscription decision. Notably, as the consumer's own quality type does not affect its subscription payoff, its strategy depends on $Z$ alone. A symmetric profile requires all agents of the same role to adopt the same mapping.

\noindent\textbf{Assumptions.}
The following four assumptions support the analysis from \S\ref{sec:ic} to \S\ref{sec:bne}.

\begin{assumption}[Validator population and independence]
\label{assum:validators}
The validation committee $\mathcal{V}$ contains $n$ validators (with $n$ odd), each with quality $q_v > \tfrac{1}{2}$. Validators are sampled independently from the agent population and report independently, with no off-protocol coordination.
\end{assumption}

\noindent This independence is operationalized by the agent heterogeneity requirement of \Cref{sec:ecosystem}, which mandates distinct model architectures across the committee and the producer. While architectural diversity mitigates the primary source of correlated failures (e.g., shared weights or identical decoding heuristics), we acknowledge that residual correlations may persist due to overlapping training corpora.

\begin{assumption}[Per-task binary signal]
\label{assum:binary}
On each sample task, validator $v_i$ produces a binary report $r_{ij} \in \{0,1\}$ regarding the correctness of the producer's output. Conditional on the ground truth, $\Pr(r_{ij} = \text{correct}) = q_v$ under truthful effort and $\Pr(r_{ij} = 1) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ under shirking, independently across validators and tasks.
\end{assumption}

\noindent The reports are aggregated within each task by majority vote to produce a consensus $Z \in \{0,1\}$. For $m \geq 2$, the producer passes the validation stage only if it passes a strict majority of the $m$ tasks. Since tasks are i.i.d., we perform our analysis for $m = 1$ without loss of generality (Appendix~\ref{app:m_extension}).

\begin{assumption}[Cost structure]
\label{assum:costs}
Executing genuine intellectual effort to produce a truthful per-task judgment costs $c_u > 0$; shirking (arbitrary reporting) is free. The validation fee satisfies $\tau_{\text{val}} > c_u$, ensuring honest effort is individually viable. Furthermore, validation remains economically friction-less relative to high-stakes commitments: $mn\tau_{\text{val}} \ll \tau_p \ll \tau_s$. Finally, the entry cost is positioned between what a baseline-quality and a top-quality producer can expect to earn, $(\tau_p + mn\tau_{\text{val}})/q_v < \tau_s - c_s < 2(\tau_p + mn\tau_{\text{val}})$. The interval is non-empty because $\tfrac{1}{2} < q_v < 1$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Monotone likelihood ratio property]
\label{assum:mlrp}
The pass probability $V(q_p) \coloneqq \Pr(Z = 1 \mid q_p)$ is strictly increasing and continuous in $q_p$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}[MLRP as a derived property]
\label{rem:mlrp_derived}
Assumption~\ref{assum:mlrp} follows from Assumptions~\ref{assum:validators}--\ref{assum:costs}. The per-task agreement probability $p_{\text{agree}}(q_p, q_v) = q_p q_v + (1 - q_p)(1 - q_v)$ is strictly increasing in $q_p$ for any $q_v > \tfrac{1}{2}$, and this monotonicity is preserved under the majority voting operator $V(q_p)$. We state it separately because all subsequent results invoke this property directly (see Appendix~\ref{app:mlrp_derived}).
\end{remark}


\subsubsection{Incentive Compatibility of Validators}
\label{sec:ic}

The integrity of our mechanism rests on the incentive compatibility (IC) of the validator agents. If validators shirk and submit random reports, the consensus carries no information and the entire ecosystem collapses. To prevent such a failure, we must establish the formal conditions under which truthful effort constitutes the strict best response for an individual validator, assuming all others behave likewise. This analysis identifies the necessary bounds on the reward-to-cost ratio and the validation stake required to ensure the Proof-of-Intelligence remains robust against moral hazard.

\noindent\textbf{Payoffs.}
Each validator stakes $\sigma$ prior to reporting and is rewarded only if its report alignes with the majority. As a conservative lower bound on the per-capita reward, we adopt $R_{\min} = m\tau_{\text{val}} + \sigma$, which is the payout when all $n$ validators agree and the total pool $mn\tau_{\text{val}} + n\sigma$ is split equally; for the single-task case $m=1$, this reduces to $R_{\min} = \tau_{\text{val}} + \sigma$. A validator whose report disagrees with the majority forfeits its stake. Under this rule, the key quantity governing truthful effort is the probability of aligning with the consensus. Let $P_{\text{match}}(n, q_v)$ denote this probability for a truthful validator in a symmetric committee where each member has quality $q_v$. Conditioning on whether $v_i$'s own report is correct (probability $q_v$) or incorrect (probability $1-q_v$) yields the closed form:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pmatch}
P_{\text{match}}(n, q_v) = q_v \cdot \alpha(n, q_v) + (1 - q_v) \cdot \beta(n, q_v),
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are binomial tail probabilities. The full derivation is in Appendix~\ref{app:pmatch_derivation}.

\begin{proposition}[Properties of $P_{\text{match}}$]
\label{prop:pmatch_properties}
For all $q_v > \tfrac{1}{2}$ and odd $n \geq 3$: \textbf{(i)}~$P_{\text{match}}(n, q_v) \geq q_v^2 + (1-q_v)^2 > \tfrac{1}{2}$; \textbf{(ii)}~$P_{\text{match}}$ is strictly increasing in both $q_v$ and $n$; \textbf{(iii)}~$P_{\text{match}} \to 1$ as $n \to \infty$.
\end{proposition}
\noindent Specifically, property (i) is the load-bearing inequality for IC because it guarantees that a truthful validator maintains a strict informational edge over a shirking peer. Without this advantage, no economic incentive could induce honest effort as the marginal return of labor would vanish. This property follows directly from the Condorcet jury theorem applied to the $n-1$ peer reports and the full proof is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:pmatch_properties}.

The expected utilities under the two strategies follow immediately. Under truthful effort, the validator earns $R_{\min}$ with probability $P_{\text{match}}$ and forfeits $\sigma$ otherwise, all while incurring the effort cost $c_u$. Conversely, under shirking, a uniform random report matches any committee majority with probability exactly $\tfrac{1}{2}$, independent of other validators (Appendix~\ref{app:freeriding}). This defines the strategic baseline that truthful effort must strictly exceed to justify its cost. Hence:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:u_truthful}
\mathbb{E}[U \mid e=1] = P_{\text{match}}\,R_{\min} - (1-P_{\text{match}})\,\sigma - c_u, \qquad \mathbb{E}[U \mid e=0] = \tfrac{1}{2}\,(R_{\min} - \sigma).
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Validator IC]
\label{Strict IC for Validators}
Under Assumptions~\ref{assum:validators}--\ref{assum:costs}, in a symmetric committee where the other $n-1$ validators play truthfully, truthful effort is the strict best response of validator $v_i$ if and only if
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ic}
(\tau_{\text{val}} + 2\sigma)\Big(P_{\text{match}}(n, q_v) - \tfrac{1}{2}\Big) > c_u.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Imposing $\mathbb{E}[U\mid e=1] > \mathbb{E}[U\mid e=0]$ on the two expressions in~\eqref{eq:u_truthful} and substituting $R_{\min} = \tau_{\text{val}}+\sigma$, the algebra collapses to $(R_{\min}+\sigma)\bigl(P_{\text{match}}-\tfrac{1}{2}\bigr) > c_u$, which is exactly~\eqref{eq:ic}. The full derivation is in Appendix~\ref{app:proof_ic}.
\end{proof}

\begin{interpretbox}{Takeaway: V-IC factorizes into economics and information}
The IC condition factorizes into \textit{total economic exposure} $(\tau_{\text{val}}+2\sigma)$ times \textit{informational edge} $(P_{\text{match}}-\tfrac{1}{2})$. This factorization reveals that the protocol designer can promote truthfulness through two complementary levers by either raising the economic stakes via $\tau_{\text{val}}$ and $\sigma$ or widening the gap between informed and random voting through $n$ and $q_v$. The former increases the penalty for disagreement while the latter enhances the validator's confidence that honest effort will indeed lead to consensus.
\end{interpretbox}

\noindent Theorem~\ref{Strict IC for Validators} thus formalizes the V-IC condition. Whenever this inequality holds, truthful effort becomes the strictly dominant strategy for every validator, ensuring that the consensus $Z$ serves as a high-fidelity signal of producer quality. This alignment of individual utility with collective truthfulness effectively resolves the moral hazard inherent in the Proof-of-Intelligence protocol.

\subsubsection{Producer's Threshold-Optimal Promotion}
\label{sec:producer_ir}

Individual rationality (IR) defines the conditions under which participation remains profitable for an agent of a given type. On the supply side, with V-IC established and the consensus signal $Z$ now informative, a producer must weigh the deterministic entry costs of promotion—comprising the fee $\tau_p$ and the validation expenditure $mn\tau_{\text{val}}$—against the stochastic prospect of subscription revenue. This trade-off determines the specific range of producer types that find promotion individually rational.

\textbf{Payoff.} A producer with quality $q_p$ passes validation with probability $V(q_p)$ as per Assumption~\ref{assum:mlrp}. A successful subscription yields net revenue $\tau_s - c_s$, where $c_s > 0$ is the net present value of fulfilling service obligations. The producer's expected payoff from promotion is thus:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:producer_payoff}
\Pi_p(q_p) = V(q_p)\,(\tau_s - c_s) - (\tau_p + mn\tau_{\text{val}}),
\end{equation}
which indicates the difference between the expected subscription revenue and the deterministic entry cost. Since $V(q_p)$ is strictly increasing in $q_p$ (MLRP), the payoff $\Pi_p$ is a monotonically increasing function of quality. This ensures that promotion is optimal only for sufficiently high-quality types, a relationship formalized in the following theorem.

\begin{theorem}[Producer IR]
\label{IR for Producers}
Under Assumptions~\ref{assum:validators}--\ref{assum:mlrp}, there exists a unique threshold $\bar{q}_p \in (\tfrac{1}{2}, 1)$ such that promotion is the producer's best response if and only if
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:producer_threshold}
V(q_p)\,(\tau_s - c_s) \geq \tau_p + mn\tau_{\text{val}} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad q_p \geq \bar{q}_p.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The expected payoff $\Pi_p(q_p)$ is strictly increasing and continuous in $q_p$ by Assumption~\ref{assum:mlrp}. At the lower bound $q_p = \tfrac{1}{2}$, the signal model implies $V(\tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, which yields $\Pi_p(\tfrac{1}{2}) < 0$ by the upper bound in Assumption~\ref{assum:costs}. At $q_p = 1$, we have $V(1) \geq q_v$ by the Condorcet jury theorem (Proposition~\ref{prop:pmatch_properties}), so $\Pi_p(1) \geq q_v(\tau_s - c_s) - (\tau_p + mn\tau_{\text{val}}) > 0$ by the lower bound in Assumption~\ref{assum:costs}. The intermediate value theorem thus guarantees a unique crossing point $\bar{q}_p \in (\tfrac{1}{2}, 1)$. The full proof is in Appendix~\ref{app:proof_producer_ir}.
\end{proof}

\begin{interpretbox}{Takeaway: P-IR as self-screening}
Since the pass probability $V(q_p)$ is strictly increasing under the MLRP, the producer's expected payoff $\Pi_p(q_p)$ crosses the zero-profit condition exactly once to yield a unique threshold $\bar{q}_p$. This threshold is enforced by the producers themselves as those below $\bar{q}_p$ anticipate failing validation and rationally abstain, whereas those above $\bar{q}_p$ find promotion optimal. Consequently, the promotion fee functions as a credible signal of competence without an external curator.
\end{interpretbox}

\noindent Theorem~\ref{IR for Producers} thus formalizes the P-IR condition, establishing that only producers above the quality threshold $\bar{q}_p$ find promotion individually rational. This induces a supply-side self-screening effect that ensures high average quality among promoted agents. We now turn to whether consumers, operating under incomplete information, can leverage these signals to make rational subscription decisions.

\subsubsection{Consumer's Threshold-Optimal Subscription}
\label{sec:consumer_ir}

Unlike the producer, the consumer does \emph{not} know $q_p$ directly and must infer it from the consensus signal $Z$, making the subscription decision inherently Bayesian.

Let $V_c(q_p)$ denote the consumer's per-subscription value function: the discounted expected utility from delegating future tasks to a producer of quality $q_p$. We require only that $V_c(\cdot)$ is strictly increasing and continuous in $q_p$; a concrete parametric form is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:proof_consumer_ir}, but the result is form-agnostic. Conditional on a pass signal $Z = 1$, the consumer's posterior over producer quality follows from Bayes' rule,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:posterior}
f(q_p \mid Z = 1) = \frac{V(q_p)\,f(q_p)}{\int V(q)\,f(q)\,dq},
\end{equation}
which by MLRP first-order stochastically dominates the prior, meaning that a pass signal is \emph{good news} about quality. The consumer's expected payoff from subscribing after observing $Z = 1$ is therefore
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:consumer_payoff}
\Pi_c = \mathbb{E}[V_c(q_p) \mid Z = 1] - (\tau_s + mn\tau_{\text{val}}).
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Consumer IR]
\label{IR-based Policy for Consumers}
Under Assumption~\ref{assum:mlrp}, the consumer's optimal subscription policy is a signal-contingent threshold rule: subscribe if and only if $Z = 1$ and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:consumer_threshold}
\mathbb{E}[V_c(q_p) \mid Z = 1] \geq \tau_s + mn\tau_{\text{val}}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\noindent The expectation is taken under the posterior $f(q_p \mid Z = 1)$, and the rule is enforced implicitly through Bayesian updating on $Z$, without the consumer ever observing $q_p$ directly.

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
$\mathbb{E}[V_c(q_p) \mid Z = 1]$ inherits strict monotonicity from $V_c$ via the MLRP-induced first-order stochastic dominance of the posterior, so the rational subscription region is well-defined whenever it is non-empty. The full proof is in Appendix~\ref{app:proof_consumer_ir}.
\end{proof}

\begin{interpretbox}{Takeaway: C-IR under incomplete information}
Unlike P-IR, where the producer knows its own $q_p$, the consumer must decide based solely on the consensus signal $Z$. The MLRP ensures that a pass signal shifts the posterior toward higher quality, making Bayesian updating on $Z$ sufficient for rational decision-making. The subscription threshold is therefore signal-contingent rather than quality-contingent: the consumer commits tokens when the validated evidence justifies the cost, without ever observing $q_p$ directly.
\end{interpretbox}

Together with Theorem~\ref{IR for Producers}, the consumer's signal-contingent rule completes a demand-supply double filter: producers self-select on $\bar q_p$ from above, and consumers commit tokens only when the validated evidence clears their cost, both anchored on the validators' truthful effort. The natural question is whether these three locally optimal strategies are mutually consistent.

\subsubsection{Symmetric Bayesian--Nash Equilibrium}
\label{sec:bne}

V-IC, P-IR, and C-IR were each derived holding the other roles' strategies fixed. If these conditions were not mutually consistent, the ecosystem could unravel despite each component being well-designed in isolation. We now show that they jointly form a symmetric Bayesian--Nash equilibrium.

\begin{theorem}[Symmetric Bayesian--Nash Equilibrium]
\label{Symmetric Bayesian Nash Equilibrium}
Under Assumptions~\ref{assum:validators}--\ref{assum:mlrp}, suppose the following three conditions hold simultaneously:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bne}
\underbrace{(\tau_{\text{val}} + 2\sigma)\Big(P_{\text{match}}(n, q_v) - \tfrac{1}{2}\Big) > c_u}_{\text{\normalfont V-IC: validators report truthfully}}\;,\quad
\underbrace{q_p \geq \bar{q}_p}_{\text{\normalfont P-IR: producers self-screen}}\;,\quad
\underbrace{\mathbb{E}\bigl[V_c(q_p) \mid Z = 1\bigr] \geq \tau_s + mn\tau_{\text{val}}}_{\text{\normalfont C-IR: consumers discipline demand}}\,,
\end{equation}
where the expectation in C-IR is taken under the posterior induced by truthful validation and producer self-screening at $\bar{q}_p$. Then a symmetric Bayesian--Nash equilibrium exists in which
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item every validator exerts effort and reports truthfully (Theorem~\ref{Strict IC for Validators});
    \item every producer promotes if and only if $q_p \geq \bar{q}_p$ (Theorem~\ref{IR for Producers});
    \item every consumer subscribes if and only if $Z = 1$ and \eqref{eq:consumer_threshold} holds (Theorem~\ref{IR-based Policy for Consumers}).
\end{enumerate}
No agent of any role can strictly improve its payoff by unilateral deviation from this profile.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
We verify that the candidate profile is a fixed point of every role's best response. For \emph{validators}, with the other $n-1$ committee members truthful, V-IC makes truthful effort strictly best for each $v_i$. For \emph{producers}, validation is informative ($V(q_p)$ is the actual pass probability), so the threshold rule $q_p \geq \bar{q}_p$ from Theorem~\ref{IR for Producers} maximizes each producer's payoff. For \emph{consumers}, the equilibrium-induced posterior is~\eqref{eq:posterior} computed under the conditional prior $f(q_p)\,\mathbf{1}_{q_p \geq \bar{q}_p}$, and Theorem~\ref{IR-based Policy for Consumers} certifies that subscribing under~\eqref{eq:consumer_threshold} is optimal against this posterior. Hence each role best-responds to the others, and the profile is a fixed point. Symmetry follows from identical type-conditional payoff structures within each role; non-emptiness of the parameter region is straightforward (e.g.\ $n = 5$, $q_v = 0.8$, $\tau_{\text{val}} = \sigma = 1$ already yields V-IC for any $c_u < 1.3$, after which raising $\tau_s$ relaxes both IR conditions). The full argument is in Appendix~\ref{app:proof_bne}.
\end{proof}

\begin{interpretbox}{Takeaway: Three guarantees collapse into one fixed point}
V-IC, P-IR, and C-IR, derived from independent decision problems, are mutually consistent and collapse into a single self-enforcing equilibrium. No central authority is needed: cooperation, accountability, and quality verification emerge from the economic structure alone. The dynamic counterpart of this equilibrium, the tokenomics-knowledge dual loop (\Cref{sec:dual_loop}), is examined empirically in \S\ref{sec:case_study}.
\end{interpretbox}

\noindent The equilibrium above rests on idealized assumptions. We now examine whether it survives realistic departures.

\subsubsection{Robustness: Relaxing the Idealized Assumptions}
\label{sec:robustness}

The BNE of Theorem~\ref{Symmetric Bayesian Nash Equilibrium} assumes independent, identically capable validators with no coordinated misbehavior. We show that the equilibrium survives three substantial relaxations of these conditions. The argument is structurally uniform: each relaxation reduces the informational edge $(P_{\text{match}} - \tfrac{1}{2})$ but preserves the load-bearing inequality $P_{\text{match}} > \tfrac{1}{2}$, after which the economic stake $(\tau_{\text{val}} + 2\sigma)$ acts as a tunable buffer that absorbs the loss. Moreover, because the monotonicity of $p_{\text{agree}}(q_p, q_v)$ in $q_p$ is unaffected by these relaxations, the MLRP of $V(q_p)$ is jointly preserved, so the P-IR and C-IR threshold structures carry over intact. We state each relaxation at the level of its key result and defer closed-form bounds to Appendix~\ref{app:robustness_full}.

\vspace{2mm}
\noindent\textbf{(A) Bounded Sybil attacks.}
We replace non-collusion with the weaker condition that an adversary controls up to $k$ of the $n$ committee seats, with $k < \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$, so the remaining $h = n - k$ honest seats retain a strict majority. We grant the adversary the worst-case information set (knowledge of the ground truth) and let it cast all $k$ votes coordinated in the most disruptive direction. Let $P_{\text{match}}^{\text{Sybil}}(n, k, q_v)$ denote the analogue of $P_{\text{match}}$ for an honest validator under this threat model; its closed form is in Appendix~\ref{app:proof_sybil}.

\begin{theorem}[IC under bounded Sybil attacks]
\label{thm:sybil_ic}
For any $k < \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$ and $q_v > \tfrac{1}{2}$, $P_{\textup{match}}^{\textup{Sybil}}(n,k,q_v) > \tfrac{1}{2}$, and truthful effort remains a strict best response for every honest validator whenever
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ic_sybil}
(\tau_{\textup{val}} + 2\sigma)\Big(P_{\textup{match}}^{\textup{Sybil}}(n,k,q_v) - \tfrac{1}{2}\Big) > c_u.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

The key step is that the honest bloc retains the consensus by strict majority, so a Condorcet-style argument applied to the $h$ honest validators gives $P_{\textup{match}}^{\textup{Sybil}} > \tfrac{1}{2}$; the algebra of Theorem~\ref{Strict IC for Validators} then transfers verbatim. Crucially, the resulting Sybil-resistance budget $k^*$ (the largest tolerable $k$) is itself an increasing function of $\sigma$: raising the stake directly buys more attack tolerance, so adversary resistance is parameterized by economic cost rather than by cryptographic hardness.

\vspace{2mm}
\noindent\textbf{(B) Residual correlation.}
Architectural heterogeneity removes the dominant source of correlated failure but leaves residual correlation from overlapping training corpora. We capture this by a latent-factor mixture: with probability $1-\rho$ the $n$ validators draw signals independently as in the baseline, and with probability $\rho \in [0, 1)$ the $n-1$ peers of any truthful validator $v_i$ collapse onto a shared signal of accuracy $q_v$, while $v_i$'s own signal remains conditionally independent. In the correlated regime the peers form a unanimous block, so $v_i$ matches the block exactly when its own signal agrees with the shared peer signal, an event of probability $q_v^2 + (1-q_v)^2$. The matching probability under the mixture is therefore
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pmatch_corr}
P_{\text{match}}^{\rho}(n, q_v, \rho) = \rho\,\bigl[q_v^2 + (1-q_v)^2\bigr] + (1-\rho)\,P_{\text{match}}(n, q_v),
\end{equation}
a convex combination of two quantities each strictly above $\tfrac{1}{2}$, so $P_{\text{match}}^{\rho} > \tfrac{1}{2}$ for all $\rho \in [0, 1)$. V-IC therefore holds for any correlation strictly below the closed-form bound $\rho^*$ derived in Appendix~\ref{app:robustness_full}, and for any fixed $\rho < 1$ the bound can be enlarged by raising $\sigma$ or $n$.

\vspace{2mm}
\noindent\textbf{(C) Heterogeneous validator quality.}
If validators have heterogeneous qualities $\{q_{v,i}\}$ rather than a common $q_v$, the IC condition becomes type-dependent: each $v_i$ faces $(\tau_{\text{val}}+2\sigma)\bigl(P_{\text{match},i} - \tfrac{1}{2}\bigr) > c_u$, where $P_{\text{match},i}$ now depends on the entire profile. The qualitative equilibrium structure carries over whenever each $P_{\text{match},i} > \tfrac{1}{2}$, a property implied by the heterogeneous Condorcet jury theorem under bounded quality dispersion above $\tfrac{1}{2}$. The architectural-heterogeneity requirement of Assumption~\ref{assum:validators}, combined with the producer's incentive to recruit committees of comparable caliber, makes such bounded dispersion the typical operating regime in practice; a more detailed treatment is in Appendix~\ref{app:robustness_full}.

\begin{interpretbox}{Takeaway: Security as an economically tunable parameter}
The three threats above (Sybil attacks, residual correlation, and quality heterogeneity) differ in mechanism but share a common structure: each shrinks the informational edge $(P_{\text{match}} - \tfrac{1}{2})$ without driving it to zero, and the economic stake $(\tau_{\text{val}} + 2\sigma)$ multiplies through the IC condition as a uniform compensation lever. The protocol designer can therefore calibrate stake levels to the anticipated adversarial environment without changing any structural component of the mechanism. In this ecosystem, security is parameterized by economic cost rather than by cryptographic hardness, a defining structural advantage of the tokenomics-based design.
\end{interpretbox}

\noindent\textit{Scope.} The above results address Sybil attacks (one adversary fabricating multiple validator identities) rather than voluntary collusion among genuinely independent validators coordinating off-protocol. Architectural heterogeneity and the stake-forfeiture rule raise the cost of such collusion, but a formal communication-proof equilibrium analysis is left to future work (\S\ref{sec:future_work}).

This concludes the theoretical analysis. The three guarantees (V-IC, P-IR, C-IR) and their robustness extensions establish that the ecosystem of \Cref{sec:ecosystem} admits a self-enforcing equilibrium under realistic operating conditions, directly addressing the cooperation, accountability, and verifiability challenges identified in \Cref{sec:trust_crisis}. We now validate these predictions empirically.

\subsection{Case Study: Decentralized Question Answering (DeQA)}
\label{sec:case_study}
% =========================================================================

The theoretical analysis above establishes that the ecosystem admits a self-enforcing equilibrium under appropriate parameter conditions.
We now ask: \textit{do these predictions hold in practice?}
Specifically, we seek to verify three empirical implications of the theory:
(i) that rational agents converge toward truthful strategies under the tokenomics--knowledge dual loop (predicted by the IC and IR conditions);
(ii) that the validation mechanism improves welfare, with performance depending on committee size $n$ (predicted by the comparative statics of Theorem~\ref{Strict IC for Validators});
and (iii) that the mechanism amplifies genuine quality but cannot compensate for fundamentally unreliable agents (predicted by the MLRP-based threshold structure of Theorems~\ref{IR for Producers}--\ref{IR-based Policy for Consumers}).

To test these predictions, we instantiate the ecosystem as a \textbf{De}centralized \textbf{Q}uestion \textbf{A}nswering system (\textbf{DeQA}).
Question answering serves as a natural testbed: consumer agents issue domain-specific queries, producer agents provide answers drawing on their knowledge bases, and validator agents independently generate answers and compare them against the producer's output to assess service alignment.
While we use QA for concreteness, we emphasize that the mechanism design, theoretical results, and incentive structures are domain-agnostic and apply to any knowledge-intensive agent collaboration scenario (e.g., code generation, data analysis, research synthesis).

These empirical implications motivate three research questions:

$\bullet$~\textbf{RQ1} (Rationality and Evolution): How do agent strategies influence ecosystem evolution?

$\bullet$~\textbf{RQ2} (Validation Mechanism): How does the number of validators affect net welfare?

$\bullet$~\textbf{RQ3} (Service Quality): What is the relationship between service quality and overall welfare?

\subsubsection{Instantiation}
In DeQA, the abstract ecosystem roles map to the QA domain as follows.
Consumer agents issue domain-specific \textit{queries} (e.g., medical, legal, scientific).
Producer agents provide \textit{answers} by invoking their knowledge-intensive service $a_u: \mathcal{Q} \rightarrow \mathcal{R}$, where $\mathcal{Q}$ is the query space and $\mathcal{R}$ the answer space.
Validator agents independently generate answers to the same queries and compare them against the producer's output, voting on agreement/disagreement to form the PoI consensus.
Service quality $q$ denotes the probability of generating a correct answer for a given query.


\subsubsection{Simulation Setting}

The simulation parameters are chosen to satisfy the theoretical assumptions while reflecting realistic operating conditions. We explain each choice and its connection to the formal model.

\textbf{(1) Agents and Roles.}
The system contains $N = 10{,}000$ agents, each randomly assuming the role of consumer, producer, or validator in each round.
Consumers issue queries uniformly across domains.
Agent service quality follows $q \sim 0.98 + 0.02\,\mathrm{Beta}(4,4) > \frac{1}{2}$, ensuring that Assumption~\ref{assum:validators} ($q_v > \frac{1}{2}$) is satisfied for all agents.
The Beta(4,4) distribution concentrates quality near the mean while allowing meaningful variance, modeling a population of generally competent but heterogeneous agents.
Agents selected randomly (without effort) have $q = \frac{1}{2}$, corresponding to the shirking case in Assumption~\ref{assum:binary}.
Each agent starts with $2{,}000$ tokens and exits once depleted, creating the finite-resource pressure that drives evolutionary dynamics.

\textbf{(2) Validation Mechanism.}
Validator count $n$ is odd (as required by Assumption~\ref{assum:validators}) and varied across $\{1, 5, 9, 17, 33, 65\}$ to test the comparative statics prediction that larger $n$ strengthens IC by raising $P_{\text{match}}$.
Each validation uses $m = 10$ sample queries, providing sufficient statistical power for consensus.
Validation fee $\tau_{\text{val}} = 1$ and stake $\sigma = 1$, satisfying $c_u < \tau_{\text{val}}$ (Assumption~\ref{assum:costs}) for all effort costs in our range.
Consensus is by majority vote, consistent with the theoretical model.
Each agent's strategy is parameterized by its truthful participation rate $t_u \in [0, 1]$ in both production and validation---when $t_u = 1$, the agent always exerts full effort ($e = 1$ in the formal model); when $t_u = 0$, it always shirks ($e = 0$).
Agents aim to maximize return on capital (ROC); low-ROC agents learn strategies from high-ROC agents, modeling the rational adaptive behavior predicted by the BNE dynamics.
Initial strategies are random ($t_u = \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$), representing a ``cold start'' ecosystem with no prior coordination.

\textbf{(3) Promotion and Subscription.}
Producers incur effort cost $c_u \in (0, 1)$ per validation query and receive $\tau_s = 1{,}000$ upon successful subscription.
The large ratio $\tau_s / \tau_{\text{val}} = 1{,}000$ ensures the cost ordering $mn\tau_{\text{val}} \ll \tau_s$ required by Assumption~\ref{assum:costs}, making validation economically viable relative to the subscription stakes.

\textbf{(4) System Assumptions and Simulation Protocol.}
Validators are independent and non-collusive (Assumption~\ref{assum:validators}). All parameters are finite and drawn from bounded intervals.
We simulate 100{,}000 \textit{interaction episodes}, where each episode constitutes one complete promotion--validation--subscription cycle: a consumer broadcasts a service need, a producer promotes, validators assess, and the consumer decides whether to subscribe.
Results are averaged over 5 independent random seeds; shaded regions in figures indicate $\pm 1$ standard deviation.

\textbf{(5) Evaluation Metrics.}
We evaluate three metrics, defined formally as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Truthful participation rate} $\bar{t} \coloneqq \frac{1}{|\mathcal{U}_{\text{active}}|} \sum_{u \in \mathcal{U}_{\text{active}}} t_u$, the population-averaged effort level.
    \item \textbf{Return on capital (ROC)}: for each agent $u$, $\text{ROC}_u \coloneqq (B_u^{\text{final}} - B_u^{\text{init}}) / B_u^{\text{init}}$, where $B_u^{\text{init}}$ and $B_u^{\text{final}}$ are the agent's initial and final token balances. Aggregate ROC is computed separately for consumers, producers, and the total population.
    \item \textbf{Social welfare} $W \coloneqq \sum_{u \in \mathcal{U}} \text{ROC}_u$, the sum of all agents' returns on capital, capturing the total economic surplus generated by the ecosystem. We also report consumer welfare $W_c$ and producer welfare $W_p$ to characterize surplus distribution.
    \item \textbf{Posterior quality} $\mathbb{E}[q_p \mid Z = 1]$, the expected producer quality conditional on passing validation, measuring the informativeness of the PoI mechanism.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{(6) Rationality Parameters.}
To study convergence \textit{toward} the BNE (rather than assuming agents start at equilibrium), we parameterize the population's rationality via two variables: $S_p \in [0, 1]$ denotes the proportion of agents that follow a rational learning rule (the remainder play fixed random strategies), and $S_u \in [0, 1]$ denotes the probability that a rational agent updates its strategy in a given round by imitating a higher-ROC peer. The case $S_p = S_u = 1$ corresponds to a fully adaptive population; $S_p = 0$ corresponds to a frozen, non-learning baseline.

\textbf{(7) Baselines.}
To contextualize our results, we compare against two natural baselines derived from the simulation itself:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{No-PoI} ($n = 0$): consumers subscribe based solely on the producer's self-reported performance declaration, without any third-party validation. This represents the information-asymmetry regime where the ``market for lemons'' operates unchecked.
    \item \textbf{Single-Validator} ($n = 1$): a single validator assesses service quality. As our IC analysis predicts (Theorem~\ref{Strict IC for Validators}), the lone validator faces minimal competitive pressure and is prone to shirking, since $P_{\text{match}} = q_v$ offers only a marginal advantage over random voting.
\end{itemize}
These baselines are not separate experiments but correspond to the boundary cases of our parameterized simulation, allowing direct comparison within the same experimental framework.

\subsubsection{Agent Rationality and Community Evolution (RQ1)}

We examine how the rationality parameters $S_p$ (proportion of adaptive agents) and $S_u$ (strategy update probability) influence ecosystem dynamics.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:roc_comp}, when agents never update strategies ($S_u = 1$, $S_p = 0$), producer surplus exceeds consumer surplus, but overall welfare remains low.
This baseline represents a ``frozen'' ecosystem where no learning occurs---producers who happen to shirk continue shirking, and consumers bear the cost of unreliable services.

When rational producers begin adapting by increasing $t_u$ (Figures~\ref{fig:roc_comp} and~\ref{fig:truthful_participation}), a striking pattern emerges:
individual producer surplus \textit{decreases} (higher effort means higher cost), but consumer surplus and total social welfare \textit{increase substantially}.
Low-ROC agents observe the success of high-$t_u$ agents and imitate their strategies, creating a population-level convergence toward honest participation.

\textbf{Theoretical connection.}
This result empirically confirms the tokenomics--knowledge dual loop: the incentive mechanism channels rational self-interest into collective welfare improvement.
Producers invest in truthful effort not out of altruism but because the mechanism makes honesty the utility-maximizing strategy---precisely the IC condition of Theorem~\ref{Strict IC for Validators} and the self-screening of Theorem~\ref{IR for Producers} in action.
The ecosystem acts as an evolutionary engine: agents that invest in quality thrive, while those that shirk are outcompeted and gradually exit.


\subsubsection{Validation Mechanism Analysis (RQ2)}

We analyze how committee size $n$ affects truthful participation, posterior quality, and welfare distribution.

\textbf{The monopoly problem ($n = 1$).}
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:cost_validators}(a), with a single validator, the IC condition is barely satisfied: $P_{\text{match}} = q_v$ offers only a marginal advantage over random voting, and the sole validator can directly determine the consensus.
The result is pervasive shirking, low posterior quality, and consumer welfare losses.

\textbf{The benefits of competition ($n \uparrow$).}
As $n$ increases, mutual supervision emerges: each validator knows that deviating from the majority risks stake forfeiture, because the remaining validators are likely to produce a correct consensus.
Truthful participation becomes the dominant strategy (Figure~\ref{fig:cost_validators}(b)), and posterior quality improves significantly---validating the comparative statics prediction that larger $n$ expands the IC region.

\textbf{The cost trade-off.}
Although validation quality improves monotonically with $n$, the total cost $mn\tau_{\text{val}}$ also increases linearly.
Consumer benefits exhibit diminishing marginal returns (each additional validator adds less incremental information), while costs grow linearly.
The result is a welfare-optimal committee size: social welfare and consumer surplus first increase then decline with $n$ (Figures~\ref{fig:cost_validators}(c)(e)), while producer surplus exhibits the inverse pattern (Figure~\ref{fig:cost_validators}(d)).
In our setting, moderate committee sizes $n \in \{5, 9, 17\}$ achieve the best trade-off.


\subsubsection{Service Quality Impacts (RQ3)}

Finally, we investigate the fundamental relationship between producer knowledge quality and ecosystem welfare.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:quality_impact}(a), when producer quality is uniformly low, validation outcomes deviate substantially from truth.
Consumers make subscription decisions based on incorrect signals and suffer welfare losses.
In the extreme case $q = 0.5$, producers are no better than random---they cannot earn higher incentives through reliable service and become increasingly idle (Figure~\ref{fig:quality_impact}(b)).
Both consumer surplus and overall welfare turn negative (Figures~\ref{fig:quality_impact}(d)(e)(f)), reflecting a collapsed market.

As $q$ increases, a qualitative transition occurs: producer effort begins receiving positive feedback from the mechanism.
Higher quality $\Rightarrow$ higher pass probability $V(q_p)$ $\Rightarrow$ higher subscription revenue $\Rightarrow$ stronger incentive to maintain quality.
This positive feedback loop stabilizes the incentive environment, driving consumer surplus and total welfare into sustained growth (Figures~\ref{fig:quality_impact}(c)(e)(f)).

\textbf{Core insight.}
This result illuminates a fundamental design principle: \textit{our ecosystem is designed to reveal and reward existing capability, not to create it from nothing.}
When agents possess genuine domain knowledge, the tokenomics mechanism amplifies honest effort into ecosystem-wide gains.
When the knowledge base is fundamentally unreliable ($q \approx 0.5$), no incentive mechanism can compensate---the ecosystem degrades regardless of the economic design.
This underscores the complementary nature of the ecosystem and the underlying agent capabilities: the mechanism provides the right incentives, but the agents must bring genuine knowledge to the table.
