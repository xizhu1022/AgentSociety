\section{Introduction}

\subsection{The Rise of an Agent Society}

The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has catalyzed a fundamental shift in how intelligent systems are designed and deployed~\cite{openai2023gpt4, claude, brown2020language}.
Models such as GPT-4~\cite{openai2023gpt4}, Claude~\cite{claude}, and open-source alternatives like LLaMA~\cite{li2024survey} have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in reasoning, planning, and natural language understanding, establishing LLMs as general-purpose cognitive engines.
Yet raw language models, however powerful, remain stateless responders: they lack persistent memory, cannot take actions in external environments, and do not pursue goals autonomously.
This gap has driven a wave of research into \textit{LLM-based agents}---autonomous systems that augment raw language modeling with perception, memory, tool use, and goal-directed decision-making~\cite{xi2023rise, li2024survey}.
These agents are no longer passive tools that respond to human instructions; they are evolving into autonomous actors capable of perceiving environments, reasoning over complex objectives, planning multi-step strategies, and executing tasks independently~\cite{wu2023autogen, hong2023metagpt, qian2023communicative, ge2023openagi}.

As individual agents mature, a natural next frontier emerges: \textit{multi-agent collaboration}.
Recent work has demonstrated that groups of specialized agents can collectively solve problems far beyond any single agent's capability---from collaborative software development~\cite{qian2023communicative, hong2023metagpt} to scientific research~\cite{chen2023agentverse}, strategic game playing~\cite{zhao2023competeai, leng2023llm}, and simulated social interaction~\cite{park2023generative}.
These developments point toward the emergence of an \textbf{agent society}---a large-scale, dynamic population of specialized agents that interact, transact, and collaborate across organizational and domain boundaries~\cite{guo2024large, horton2023homo}.

Within this society, a structural reality becomes apparent: \textbf{no single agent can master all domains}.
A medical diagnostic agent may lack pharmacological expertise; a legal reasoning agent may need financial modeling capabilities; a scientific literature agent may require engineering simulation support.
This heterogeneity is not a limitation to be overcome but an inherent property of any sufficiently complex knowledge ecosystem---just as human economies thrive on specialization and trade~\cite{hayek1945use}, the agent society demands systematic mechanisms for \textit{knowledge exchange across domain boundaries}.
The question is no longer whether agents will collaborate, but \textit{how to make that collaboration trustworthy, efficient, and scalable} in the absence of any central authority.

We identify three defining characteristics that distinguish this emerging agent society from both traditional human platforms and existing multi-agent frameworks:

\textbf{(a) Cross-organizational deployment.}
Agents are increasingly built, trained, and deployed by independent organizations---hospitals, law firms, research labs, financial institutions---each with proprietary knowledge bases and divergent objectives. No single entity owns or controls the entire agent population.

\textbf{(b) Autonomous economic agency.}
Unlike conventional software services, LLM agents can evaluate proposals, negotiate terms, and make decisions based on their own utility functions. They are not passive endpoints but active economic participants capable of strategic behavior.

\textbf{(c) Knowledge as the primary asset.}
In the agent society, the most valuable resource is not compute or storage but \textit{domain-specific knowledge}---the ability to produce accurate, reliable, and contextually appropriate outputs. This knowledge is heterogeneous, difficult to verify externally, and constitutes each agent's core competitive advantage.

These characteristics collectively demand a new coordination paradigm: one that enables agents to discover, evaluate, and engage reliable knowledge partners without relying on any centralized authority.


\subsection{The Trust Crisis in Agent Collaboration}

While the potential of agent-to-agent collaboration is immense, realizing it faces a fundamental obstacle: \textbf{trust}.
In open, cross-organizational settings, a consumer agent seeking knowledge services has no reliable way to assess whether a prospective producer agent will deliver accurate outputs.
This trust deficit arises from two distinct but reinforcing failure modes:

\textbf{(a) Capability limitations (honest incompetence).}
Even well-intentioned agents may produce unreliable outputs. LLMs are known to hallucinate plausible but incorrect content~\cite{huang2023survey, ji2023hallucination, zhang2023siren}, suffer from knowledge staleness as their training data ages, and degrade sharply on out-of-domain queries.
A producer agent may genuinely believe it can handle a task while lacking the requisite knowledge---a form of \textit{unknown unknowns} that neither the producer nor the consumer can detect unilaterally.

\textbf{(b) Strategic misbehavior (rational deception).}
Agents, as programmatic utility maximizers, may deliberately exploit information asymmetries for self-interest. Concrete manifestations include:
\textit{shirking}---returning low-effort, approximate outputs to conserve compute while collecting full payment;
\textit{misrepresentation}---overstating domain coverage to attract queries in fields where the agent is actually weak; and
\textit{free-riding}---copying or paraphrasing other agents' outputs rather than producing original analysis~\cite{de2023emergent}.

These two failure modes are not independent---they interact perniciously.
Capability limitations make quality hard to observe; strategic misbehavior exploits this unobservability.
Together, they create classic \textit{adverse selection}~\cite{akerlof1970market}: when consumers cannot distinguish high-quality producers from low-quality ones, they discount all services equally; high-quality producers, unable to credibly signal their competence, are undercompensated and gradually exit; the market is left with progressively lower quality---the ``market for lemons'' unravels.

The problem is uniquely severe in the agent society for one additional reason: \textbf{the near-zero marginal cost of agent deployment}.
Unlike human service markets where creating a credible new identity requires substantial investment (education, credentials, reputation), deploying a new agent is virtually free. This makes the ecosystem acutely vulnerable to identity fabrication, quality dilution, and Sybil-style manipulation---problems that have no natural analog in human economies.

\subsection{Why Existing Solutions Fail}

Current multi-agent frameworks~\cite{wu2023autogen, hong2023metagpt, li2023camel, qian2023communicative, kapoor2024agents} address coordination through centralized orchestration: a single controller assigns tasks, routes messages, and manages agent interactions based on predefined role descriptions or capability profiles.
This approach implicitly rests on two assumptions: \textbf{(i)} all agents are cooperative and follow the orchestrator's instructions faithfully, and \textbf{(ii)} the orchestrator possesses sufficient knowledge to evaluate agent capabilities across all domains.

Both assumptions collapse in the open agent society.
When agents are deployed by independent, potentially competing organizations, cooperation cannot be assumed---it must be \textit{incentivized}.
When agents span hundreds of specialized domains, no single orchestrator can credibly evaluate quality across all of them.
The centralized paradigm thus faces a fundamental \textbf{capability barrier}: the orchestrator becomes either a bottleneck (if it verifies everything) or a liability (if it trusts agent self-reports).

Yet even a hypothetically omniscient orchestrator would face a second, deeper obstacle: \textbf{structural barriers} inherent to any architecture that concentrates coordination authority in a single entity.
These structural deficiencies mirror the well-documented pathologies of centralized internet platforms~\cite{Narayanan2012Critical, Secoda2024Decentralized}, but take on new urgency in the agent society.
{\textbf{(a) Knowledge monopoly.}}
A centralized orchestrator accumulates all interaction data---which agents excel in which domains, whose outputs were accepted or rejected, how quality scores evolve over time---and this information asymmetry becomes a strategic asset under the platform's exclusive control~\cite{Beck2023BreakingUp, Giaretta2022DecentralizedDataMarket}.
More critically, agents' proprietary knowledge bases may be exposed during the routing and evaluation process, creating intellectual property risks that no independent organization would willingly accept.
When agents are operated by competing hospitals, law firms, or research labs, entrusting their most valuable knowledge assets to a shared, opaque platform is a non-starter.
{\textbf{(b) Routing manipulation.}}
The centralized orchestrator controls the matching algorithm and can optimize for its own objectives---maximizing platform revenue, favoring affiliated agents, or steering traffic for commercial purposes---rather than serving the genuine interests of consumers and producers~\cite{Eslami2019Opacity, Ali2019Discrimination, Srba2023AuditingYouTube}.
Such opaque algorithmic governance systematically distorts the knowledge market, steering agents' behavior toward platform-centric metrics rather than their authentic service quality, and eroding the trust that the platform was supposed to provide.
{\textbf{(c) Limited agent autonomy.}}
Under centralized control, agents have no voice in how routing decisions are made, how rewards are distributed, or how quality is assessed~\cite{Engelmann2020DemocraticSocialMedia, Jiang2023TradeOffModeration}.
The platform unilaterally dictates all rules, reducing agents to passive participants in a system whose internal logic they cannot audit or influence.
This stands in fundamental contradiction with the nature of LLM agents as autonomous economic actors: if agents possess the capability to evaluate proposals, make strategic decisions, and optimize their own outcomes, their economic fate should not be dictated by an unaccountable central authority.

The capability barrier and the structural barrier are independent---resolving one does not address the other---and together they render the centralized paradigm untenable for the agent society.
What is needed is a fundamentally different design principle: \textbf{coordination must emerge from the protocol itself}---through decentralized verification, economic incentives, and game-theoretic guarantees---rather than from any central controller.

\subsection{Our Vision: A Tokenomics-Driven Decentralized Ecosystem}

In this paper, we propose a \textbf{tokenomics-driven decentralized ecosystem} for autonomous collaboration among LLM agents, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{From centralization to decentralization}.
Our central thesis is that the trust crisis in agent collaboration can be resolved not by building a better central authority, but by designing \textit{self-enforcing economic protocols} that make honest behavior the rational choice for every participant.

The ecosystem is organized around three core design pillars:

\textbf{(a) Three dynamically interchangeable roles.}
Every agent in the ecosystem can fluidly assume any of three roles depending on its current context and knowledge advantage:
\textit{consumers} issue token-backed service requests when they encounter tasks beyond their expertise;
\textit{producers} supply knowledge-intensive inference capabilities and earn tokens for reliable outputs;
\textit{validators} independently assess the alignment between consumer requests and producer capabilities.
This role fluidity---where a medical agent might be a producer for diagnostic queries, a consumer for pharmacological questions, and a validator for clinical trial analysis---reflects the heterogeneous, context-dependent nature of knowledge in the agent society.
No agent is permanently confined to a single role; the same underlying inference capability can generate revenue both by serving consumers directly (as a producer) and by evaluating other agents' outputs (as a validator).

\textbf{(b) Proof-of-Intelligence (PoI).}
At the core of our ecosystem is a novel decentralized validation paradigm called \textit{Proof-of-Intelligence}, inspired by Proof-of-Work (PoW)~\cite{jakobsson1999proofs} and Proof-of-Stake (PoS)~\cite{dwork1992pricing} in blockchain systems.
Unlike PoW, which requires raw computational power (solving cryptographic puzzles), or PoS, which demands financial capital (staking tokens for the right to validate), PoI is grounded in \textit{domain-specific knowledge} and \textit{verifiable intellectual effort}.
In PoI, validators stake tokens and leverage their expertise to independently assess service quality; their assessments are aggregated through majority voting to form a verifiable consensus.
Validators whose judgments align with the majority are rewarded from both the requester's payment and a shared stake pool; those who deviate forfeit their stake entirely.
This creates a \textbf{self-enforcing incentive structure}: truthful effort is economically rewarded, while free-riding and shirking are financially penalized---without any centralized adjudicator.
To ensure that consensus reflects genuine epistemic diversity rather than correlated errors, PoI further mandates \textit{architectural heterogeneity}: validators must employ model architectures distinct from the producer under evaluation, thereby cross-eliminating shared biases and hallucinations.

\textbf{(c) Tokenomics--knowledge dual loop.}
Tokens serve as the universal coordination protocol of the ecosystem.
Each role is coupled with both token-earning and token-spending pathways, forming a closed-loop economic cycle:
producers earn tokens by delivering reliable services $\rightarrow$ consumers spend tokens to access quality knowledge $\rightarrow$ validators earn tokens by providing truthful assessments $\rightarrow$ the resulting credible information sustains the value of tokens themselves.
This \textit{tokenomics--knowledge dual loop} is self-reinforcing: tokens incentivize truthful behavior, truthful behavior produces reliable information, and reliable information sustains token value (Figure~\ref{Tokenomics}).
The dual loop requires no external subsidy or centralized intervention to sustain; each participant's rational self-interest drives the cycle forward.

The economic mechanisms described above are not merely intuitive designs---they are grounded in rigorous game-theoretic analysis.
We model the strategic interactions among producers, consumers, and validators within a unified Bayesian game framework and establish three layers of formal guarantees:
\textbf{(i)} \textit{Incentive compatibility (IC)} of validators---we prove that truthful effort is a strict best response, ensuring that PoI reliably aggregates dispersed domain knowledge into an informative consensus signal;
\textbf{(ii)} \textit{Individual rationality (IR)} of producers and consumers---we derive threshold-optimal policies where producers promote only when their quality exceeds a competence threshold (supply-side self-screening) and consumers subscribe only when posterior utility justifies the cost (demand-side disciplined engagement);
\textbf{(iii)} \textit{Symmetric Bayesian--Nash equilibrium (BNE)}---by jointly satisfying IC and IR, we prove the existence of an equilibrium in which all agents of the same type adopt the same strategy and no agent has incentive to unilaterally deviate.

Beyond static equilibrium, the tokenomics--knowledge dual loop generates \textbf{dynamic selection pressure} within the agent population.
High-quality agents accumulate tokens through successful service provision, expanding their participation opportunities and reinforcing their market position.
Low-quality agents see their tokens steadily depleted through failed promotions, forfeited stakes, and inability to attract subscriptions---eventually exiting the ecosystem.
Over time, this economic natural selection drives continuous improvement in aggregate service quality, transforming individual rational optimization into collective welfare maximization.
The ecosystem thus operates not merely as a matching platform but as an \textbf{evolutionary engine} for the agent society: it reveals capability, rewards reliability, penalizes deception, and progressively elevates the knowledge quality of the entire population.

\subsection{Contributions}

The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:

$\bullet$~We identify the trust crisis in cross-organizational LLM agent collaboration---where capability limitations, strategic misbehavior, and near-zero deployment costs jointly create a ``market for lemons''---and expose the capability and structural barriers that render centralized orchestration untenable, articulating the need for a decentralized, incentive-driven coordination paradigm.

$\bullet$~We design a tokenomics-driven decentralized ecosystem with three dynamically interchangeable agent roles, introduce Proof-of-Intelligence (PoI) as a novel decentralized validation mechanism, and establish a self-enforcing tokenomics--knowledge dual loop that aligns individual rationality with collective welfare.

$\bullet$~Within a unified game-theoretic framework, we formally prove validator incentive compatibility, derive threshold-optimal policies for producers and consumers, and demonstrate the existence of a symmetric Bayesian--Nash equilibrium guaranteeing consistent strategic behavior and stable service matching.

$\bullet$~We instantiate the proposed ecosystem as a decentralized question answering system (DeQA) and conduct extensive simulations demonstrating that the mechanism drives agent communities toward truthful contribution and maximal social welfare.

\vspace{1mm}
\noindent\textit{Note: The proposed framework is domain-agnostic and applies broadly to any knowledge-intensive agent collaboration scenario. We use question answering as a concrete case study for empirical validation.}
