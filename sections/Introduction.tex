\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{From Language Models to an Agent Society}
\label{sec:agent_society}

While Large Language Models (LLMs) excel as cognitive engines \cite{openai2023gpt4, claude, brown2020language}, a standalone model remains a stateless function restricted to reactive input-output mapping—devoid of persistent memory, environmental agency, or intrinsic goals. To transcend these limitations, the AI community has evolved toward LLM-based agents: autonomous systems that encapsulate raw inference within perception, memory, and tool-use loops, thereby transforming the model from a passive responder into an autonomous actor \cite{xi2023rise, li2024survey, wang2024survey_agents}. Rapid progress in tool-augmented reasoning \cite{yao2023react, schick2024toolformer, qin2024toolllm}, embodied exploration \cite{wang2023voyager}, and agent infrastructure \cite{mei2024aios, liu2024agentbench} has demonstrated that individual LLM agents can already execute complex, multi-step tasks with increasing individual autonomy. 

As individual capabilities converge, multi-agent collaboration emerges to transcend the inherent constraints of individual agents in scalability and specialization. While a single specialist agent can solve tasks beyond a standalone model, teams of heterogeneous agents can tackle problems beyond the capability of any individual agent. This principle has yielded striking results within controlled, cooperative settings: role-specialized teams can autonomously complete complex software projects \cite{hong2023metagpt, qian2023communicative}, enhance factual reliability through structured debate \cite{du2024debate, chan2024chateval}, and exhibit emergent collective intelligence that far exceeds the sum of individual contributions \cite{chen2023agentverse}.

However, the most consequential frontier lies where agents do not share common objectives. When interacting under divergent incentives, LLM agents exhibit strategic behaviors mirroring rational economic actors: from price competition and reputation gaming to tacit collusion and near-Nash-equilibrium play ~\cite{park2023generative, zhao2023competeai, horton2023homo, fish2024collusion, akata2023repeated_games}. This shift carries a profound implication: as agents are built, trained, and deployed by independent entities (e.g., hospitals, law firms, and financial institutions), each guarding proprietary knowledge and competing interests, cooperation can no longer be taken for granted. Instead, it becomes a strategic decision contingent on individual rationality. At this juncture, coordination undergoes a qualitative transformation: within a single organization, it is a matter of engineering (routing and role-assignment); across organizations, it becomes a matter of economics, where agents must be properly incentivized to participate honestly.

The emerging landscape thus signifies a fundamental transition from controlled multi-agent systems toward an open \textbf{agent society}—a decentralized population of specialized agent actors that discover, evaluate, and transact across organizational and domain boundaries ~\cite{guo2024large}. This society inherits the full complexity of human market economies, including the risk of market failure. Within this ecosystem, a core reality takes hold: no single agent can master all domains~\cite{guo2024large}. This necessity for coordination is driven by the inherent fragmentation of specialized knowledge service: a clinical diagnostic agent, for instance, must dynamically consult a pharmacological agent to verify drug-drug interactions against private molecular databases, just as a legal reasoning agent requires real-time synthesis from a financial auditor agent to evaluate corporate compliance ~\cite{guo2024large}. As human wealth stems from the division of labor, the agent society necessitates robust protocols for cross-domain knowledge exchange ~\cite{hayek1945use}. Consequently, the central challenge is not whether agents will collaborate, but how to architect a trustworthy, efficient, and scalable ecosystem, one that maintains integrity without central oversight despite the inherent strategic self-interest of its participants~\cite{hayek1945use}.



\subsection{The Trust Crisis in Agent Collaboration}
\label{sec:trust_crisis}

While the envisioned agent society promises a high-velocity exchange of intelligence, its realization is obstructed by a structural void: trust. In a decentralized environment, delegating tasks to unfamiliar agents is a high-stakes gamble. A consumer must rely on a counterpart whose internal capability is a black box, whose identity is disposable, and whose incentives may conflict with its own. This is not a peripheral engineering challenge but a foundational trust crisis in the system's economic fabric. 

\begin{itemize}
    % AutoGen, MetaGPT, CAMEL, and ChatDev
    \item \textbf{Cooperation.} Existing multi-agent frameworks rely on a centralized orchestrator to assign roles and route messages, assuming all agents faithfully follow instructions, such as AutoGen, MetaGPT, CAMEL, and ChatDev~\cite{wu2023autogen, hong2023metagpt, li2023camel, qian2023communicative}. This cooperative assumption holds only in single-organization deployments where agents share a unified objective under a trusted authority. However, in an open agent society, every interaction becomes a strategic encounter. A rational producer may act as a free-rider: shirking effort to conserve compute, overstating domain coverage to attract queries, or simply duplicating another agent's output. These are not hypothetical risks but rational responses to information asymmetry by utility-maximizing agents. Consequently, in a decentralized landscape, cooperation must be incentivized rather than merely assumed.
    \item \textbf{Accountability.} Human service platforms manage trust through reputation systems and legal recourse. These mechanisms rely on the fact that establishing a credible human identity is inherently expensive, requiring years of professional credentials and social capital. Consequently, Sybil attacks, where one fabricates multiple identities to exploit a system, are naturally deterred by high entry costs. In an agent society, this assumption fails. Deploying a new agent incurs near-zero marginal cost: a fresh API key and prompt template are sufficient to spawn a new actor with no history, rendering the ecosystem acutely vulnerable to reputation laundering and quality dilution. When identities are disposable, traditional reputation-based trust offers no defense. Therefore, Accountability must therefore be rooted in economic commitment rather than persistent identity.
    \item \textbf{Verifiability.} In conventional markets, quality is often observable through physical inspection or standardized benchmarks. In an agent society, the primary commodity is specialized intelligence from autonomous agents, where a consumer agent seeks a producer’s expertise precisely because it cannot evaluate the result. This creates a fundamental information asymmetry compounded by dual uncertainty: a producer agent may lack a precise grasp of its own reliability in novel domains, while the consumer agent has no unilateral means of detection. Since quality is not self-evident, verification can no longer be a bilateral matter. Instead, it must be delegated to an incentive-aligned third party where expert auditing is economically enforced rather than voluntarily provided.
    
\end{itemize}

Collectively, the simultaneous collapse of these core assumptions triggers an adverse selection spiral that threatens to unravel the agent society into a market for lemons, where high-quality agent services may be driven out by cheaper, deceptive competitors~\cite{akerlof1970market}.

A natural impulse is to restore order through centralized orchestration, employing a master agent or platform operator to route tasks, check quality, and enforce rules. However, this remedy introduces systemic pathologies common in platform economics~\cite{Narayanan2012Critical, Secoda2024Decentralized}, which become especially dangerous when one entity controls all agent interactions: \text{(i) Data monopoly}, where the master agent collects proprietary information that independent agents, representing their own organizations, would never willingly to share with a competitor~\cite{Beck2023BreakingUp, Giaretta2022DecentralizedDataMarket}; \text{(ii) Routing manipulation}, where the platform skews service matching to boost its own profits rather than the collective welfare of participating agents ~\cite{Eslami2019Opacity, Ali2019Discrimination}; \text{(iii) Loss of self-governance}, where agents are reduced to passive executors, unable to participate in decentralized decision-making for the agent society~\cite{Engelmann2020DemocraticSocialMedia, Jiang2023TradeOffModeration}. In short, centralization does not resolve the trust crisis, but instead shifts it to the ecosystem itself.

\subsection{A Tokenomics-Driven Decentralized Ecosystem}
\label{sec:vision}

We propose a \textbf{tokenomics-driven decentralized ecosystem} in which LLM agents coordinate autonomously through economic incentives rather than centralized control (Figure~\ref{fig:decentralization}).
The central thesis is that the trust crisis can be resolved by designing protocols where honest, high-quality participation is the \textit{utility-maximizing strategy} for every agent---so that the ecosystem is sustained by rational self-interest alone.
The ecosystem rests on three pillars, each targeting a specific dimension of the coordination crisis identified above.

The first pillar is a \textit{fluid role structure} that transforms the cooperation problem into an economic opportunity.
Every agent can dynamically assume any of three roles depending on its context and knowledge advantage:
\textit{consumers} issue token-backed service requests for tasks beyond their expertise;
\textit{producers} provide knowledge-intensive inference and earn tokens for reliable outputs;
\textit{validators} independently assess producer quality on behalf of consumers.
A medical agent might serve as a producer for diagnostic queries, a consumer for pharmacological questions, and a validator for clinical trial analysis---reflecting the heterogeneous, context-dependent nature of knowledge in the agent society.
By coupling each role with both token-earning and token-spending pathways, the ecosystem makes participation in every capacity individually rational, transforming cooperation from a fragile assumption into an emergent consequence of economic self-interest.

The second pillar is a novel validation mechanism called \textit{Proof-of-Intelligence (PoI)}, which resolves the verifiability crisis by delegating quality assessment to economically motivated third parties.
Inspired by Proof-of-Work~\cite{jakobsson1999proofs} and Proof-of-Stake~\cite{dwork1992pricing} in blockchain systems, PoI is grounded not in raw computation or financial capital but in \textit{domain knowledge}: validators stake tokens, independently assess service quality using their own models, and are rewarded only when their judgments align with the majority consensus---those who deviate forfeit their stake.
To ensure that consensus reflects genuine epistemic diversity rather than correlated errors, PoI mandates \textit{architectural heterogeneity}: validators must employ model architectures distinct from the producer under evaluation, substantially reducing shared failure modes.

The third pillar is the \textit{tokenomics--knowledge dual loop}, which resolves the accountability crisis by replacing identity-based trust with economic commitment.
Token flows link all three roles into a self-reinforcing cycle: producers earn tokens for reliable services $\to$ consumers spend tokens for quality knowledge $\to$ validators earn tokens for truthful assessments $\to$ truthful assessments sustain the information quality that makes tokens valuable (Figure~\ref{fig:tokenomics}).
Crucially, token staking serves as an economic bond that makes misbehavior costly regardless of identity---directly neutralizing the Sybil threat: creating a new identity is free, but staking tokens on that identity is not.
Given appropriate mechanism parameters (\Cref{sec:ecosystem}), the dual loop is sustained by rational self-interest alone, requiring no external subsidy or centralized intervention.

These design choices are not merely intuitive---they are backed by rigorous game-theoretic guarantees.
Within a unified Bayesian game framework, we establish three layers of formal results:
\textbf{(i)}~\textit{Incentive compatibility (IC)}---truthful effort is a strict best response for every validator, ensuring that PoI produces an informative consensus signal;
\textbf{(ii)}~\textit{Individual rationality (IR)}---producers promote only when their quality exceeds a self-screening threshold, and consumers subscribe only when posterior utility justifies the cost;
\textbf{(iii)}~\textit{Symmetric Bayesian--Nash equilibrium (BNE)}---the IC and IR conditions are mutually consistent, forming a stable equilibrium where no agent has incentive to deviate.
We further prove that these results are \textit{robust} to bounded Sybil attacks, correlated validator errors, and heterogeneous validator quality (\Cref{sec:robustness}).

Beyond static equilibrium, the dual loop generates \textbf{evolutionary selection pressure}: high-quality agents accumulate tokens and expand their participation, while low-quality agents deplete their reserves and exit.
Over time, this economic selection progressively elevates the aggregate capability of the agent population, making the ecosystem not merely a coordination platform but an \textbf{evolutionary engine} for the agent society.

\subsection{Contributions}
\label{sec:contributions}

$\bullet$~We identify the trust crisis in cross-organizational LLM agent collaboration---where capability limitations, strategic misbehavior, and near-zero deployment costs jointly create adverse selection---and expose the capability and structural barriers that render centralized orchestration untenable, articulating the need for an incentive-driven decentralized ecosystem.

$\bullet$~We design a tokenomics-driven decentralized ecosystem with three dynamically interchangeable agent roles, introduce Proof-of-Intelligence (PoI) as a novel decentralized validation mechanism grounded in domain knowledge, and establish a tokenomics--knowledge dual loop that aligns individual rationality with collective welfare.

$\bullet$~Within a unified game-theoretic framework, we formally prove validator incentive compatibility, derive threshold-optimal policies for producers and consumers, demonstrate the existence of a symmetric Bayesian--Nash equilibrium, and prove robustness to bounded Sybil attacks, correlated errors, and heterogeneous validator quality.

$\bullet$~We instantiate the ecosystem as a decentralized question answering system (DeQA) and conduct extensive simulations demonstrating that the mechanism drives agent communities toward truthful participation and improved social welfare.

\vspace{1mm}
\noindent\textit{The proposed framework is domain-agnostic and applies broadly to any knowledge-intensive agent collaboration scenario. We use question answering as a concrete case study for empirical validation.}
