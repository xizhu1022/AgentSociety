\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations_and_future_work}

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

We acknowledge several limitations that mark the boundary of what the current ecosystem and mechanism design can claim.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=4pt, topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Architectural heterogeneity in practice.} Our mechanism relies on validator committees architecturally distinct from the producer to reduce correlated errors. Real-world ecosystems, however, may converge on a small number of dominant model families such as GPT, Claude, and Llama derivatives, turning architectural diversity into a scarce economic resource rather than the free assumption the independence proof relies on.

    \item \textbf{Scalar and binary quality signal.} PoI represents agent quality as a scalar and aggregates validator reports into a binary verdict. Real-world service quality is multi-dimensional, covering correctness, completeness, pedagogical quality, and domain-specific nuance, which the current pass/fail aggregation cannot distinguish and therefore cannot price differentially.

    \item \textbf{Blockchain deployment not concretely specified.} The mechanism maps cleanly onto blockchain primitives such as decentralized ledgers, smart contracts, and on-chain consensus, but we treat the underlying infrastructure as a black box rather than analyzing real-world deployment trade-offs. Gas costs, settlement latency, and the choice among Layer-1, Layer-2, or dedicated validation layers each materially affect the mechanism's economic viability in practice.

    \item \textbf{Validation overhead at scale.} Each promotion and subscription runs multiple validators on multiple tasks, adding latency and compute cost. For high-throughput or latency-sensitive applications such as real-time trading or emergency consultation, this overhead is a real scalability bottleneck that the present design does not try to amortize.

    \item \textbf{Simulation-based empirical evidence.} Our DeQA case study uses parameterized synthetic agents rather than live LLM deployments, isolating the mechanism dynamics cleanly but leaving open whether the predicted patterns survive in practice. Field studies with heterogeneous open-source and proprietary agents are needed before the incentive predictions can be trusted to transfer across real-world inference variability and adversarial behavior.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future Work}
\label{sec:future_work}

Looking beyond these limitations, we envision several broader directions that extend the ecosystem toward its longer-term potential.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=4pt, topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Richer Trust Substrate.} Our mechanism binds trust to a single per-transaction stake with no memory across interactions. Layering stake with on-chain reputation and portable decentralized identity would let persistent honesty earn cumulative credit and trust travel with agents across the broader agent society. The central challenge is preventing reputation laundering, where agents farm credit through low-stake honesty and cash it out in high-stake deception, hollowing the very trust signal the system is meant to protect.

    \item \textbf{Privacy-Preserving and Verifiable Validation.} PoI currently requires validators to openly execute inference and compare outputs, which clashes with the privacy demands of healthcare, law, and finance. Cryptographic techniques such as trusted execution environments, zero-knowledge machine learning, and secure multi-party computation could let validators attest to honest inference without revealing model weights, user queries, or intermediate reasoning traces. The fundamental tension is that auditability demands transparency while privacy demands opacity, and PoI needs both at the scale of live consensus.

    \item \textbf{Cross-Ecosystem Interoperability.} A single PoI ecosystem is one cell in the larger agent society we envision, and realizing that vision requires agents, reputations, and economic commitments to travel across independently governed ecosystems. Primitives such as stake bridging, portable reputation attestations, and cross-chain identity would let an agent's track record in one ecosystem count toward its standing in another while preserving each local mechanism's incentive structure. The open problem is exposing a consistent trust surface to outsiders without forcing every ecosystem onto a single common protocol, a uniformity the decentralization ethos itself rejects.

    \item \textbf{Cold-Start and Ecosystem Bootstrapping.} The tokenomics-knowledge dual loop is self-sustaining only past critical mass and cannot ignite from an empty state, since validators await producers, producers await consumers, and no role rationally moves first. Bootstrap strategies such as trusted consortium seeding, initial token subsidies, and commitments that amortize early validation costs across future transactions can tip the system past that threshold. The open question is how much external scaffolding an ecosystem can absorb before its decentralization promise erodes into a disguised centralized launch.

    \item \textbf{Mature-Ecosystem Governance.} A long-lived agent society outgrows its initial configuration and must evolve its rules, economic parameters, and the underlying Proof-of-Intelligence protocol over time. Decentralized governance provides the natural vehicle, though on-chain voting brings familiar pathologies of plutocratic capture and low participation. Designing governance that remains responsive, legitimate, and capture-resistant across an ecosystem's full lifespan is a defining open problem for decentralized infrastructure at scale.

    \item \textbf{Theoretical Extensions.} Our game-theoretic analysis rests on a single-shot, static-quality abstraction that deliberately simplifies the richer dynamics of real ecosystems. Natural extensions include dynamic quality that evolves with validation feedback, communication-proof equilibria beyond the bounded Sybil setting, repeated-game treatments with endogenous reputation and learning, and an independent empirical study of consumer individual rationality. Each refines a modeling assumption without altering the mechanism, letting theoretical refinement and mechanism deployment advance on separable but mutually informed tracks.
\end{itemize}
