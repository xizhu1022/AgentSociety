\section{Related Works}

\textbf{Multi-Agent LLM Systems.}
The rapid advancement of LLMs has catalyzed a proliferation of multi-agent frameworks for collaborative task solving.
AutoGen~\cite{wu2023autogen} enables flexible multi-agent conversation with customizable roles and human-in-the-loop capabilities.
MetaGPT~\cite{hong2023metagpt} encodes human-like standard operating procedures into multi-agent collaboration, assigning specialized software engineering roles (product manager, architect, engineer) to coordinating agents.
CAMEL~\cite{li2023camel} explores autonomous cooperation through role-playing with inception prompting, while ChatDev~\cite{qian2023communicative} models software development as a multi-agent chat chain where agents in distinct roles communicate through structured dialogue.
AgentVerse~\cite{chen2023agentverse} investigates emergent group behaviors when multiple agents collaborate dynamically, and recent surveys~\cite{guo2024large} comprehensively map the landscape of LLM-based multi-agent systems.
CompeteAI~\cite{zhao2023competeai} studies competitive dynamics among LLM agents in market simulations, revealing emergent economic behaviors.
However, all these frameworks share a common architectural assumption: agents are cooperative and governed by a single orchestrator that manages task assignment and message routing.
None addresses the fundamental challenges of cross-organizational trust, strategic misbehavior, or incentive misalignment that arise when agents are deployed by independent entities with divergent objectives---the core challenges our ecosystem is designed to resolve.
Our work complements these frameworks by providing the missing \textit{economic coordination layer} that enables trustworthy collaboration among self-interested agents.


\textbf{Question Answering Systems.}
Question answering (QA) has evolved from retrieval-based pipelines using TF-IDF~\cite{aizawa2003information} and BM25~\cite{robertson2009probabilistic} to neural approaches that integrate retrieval and comprehension end-to-end~\cite{chen2017reading}.
The advent of LLMs has further transformed QA: models such as GPT-4 achieve strong zero- and few-shot performance through prompt-based inference, while Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) improves factual grounding by combining neural retrievers with generators~\cite{schwartz2023enhancing}.
Multi-agent QA systems have also emerged: Aryal et al.~\cite{aryal2024multiagent} propose a framework where domain-specific agents collaborate for cross-domain knowledge discovery.
However, existing QA systems---whether centralized or multi-agent---do not address incentive alignment, quality verification under information asymmetry, or economic coordination among independently deployed agents.
Our case study (DeQA) instantiates the proposed ecosystem in the QA domain, demonstrating that tokenomics-driven decentralized coordination can enhance both the reliability and sustainability of knowledge exchange.


\textbf{Decentralized Systems.}
Decentralized architectures have redefined digital coordination by enabling trustless value exchange without central authority.
From peer-to-peer sharing to blockchain-based smart contracts, platforms like Ethereum~\cite{buterin2014ethereum} introduced programmable logic and token incentives to power autonomous applications.
Recent architectures adopt rollups and data availability layers~\cite{celestia2025data} for scalability, while decentralized storage ensures verifiable and persistent data access~\cite{trautwein2022ipfs}.
At the intersection of decentralization and AI, Bittensor~\cite{bittensor2023} creates a token-incentivized peer-to-peer market for machine intelligence, and Petals~\cite{borzunov2023petals} enables collaborative decentralized inference by distributing model layers across volunteer nodes.
However, these systems focus primarily on model training or inference infrastructure rather than on the higher-level problem of \textit{runtime agent collaboration}---coordinating which agent should serve which task, verifying quality, and distributing rewards.
Our work extends decentralized coordination to this knowledge-intensive agent ecosystem layer, where tokens regulate not just access but also validation, routing, and participation quality.


\textbf{Economic Incentives and Mechanism Design.}
Economic incentives are central to decentralized system viability.
Web3 protocols embed coordination into infrastructure via programmable tokens---a paradigm known as \textit{tokenomics}---governing token issuance, distribution, and utility~\cite{ito2024cryptoeconomics, abadi2024governance, zuo2023set}.
Staking secures consensus~\cite{cong2025tokenomics}, liquidity mining supports decentralized exchanges~\cite{augustin2024yield, liu2022two-swaps}, and governance tokens incentivize protocol stewardship~\cite{abadi2024governance}.
Information elicitation mechanisms such as prediction markets~\cite{wolfers2004prediction} and peer prediction~\cite{miller2005eliciting} provide theoretical foundations for truthful reporting without ground truth---a problem directly relevant to our PoI mechanism, where validators must be incentivized to report honestly despite the absence of verifiable correct answers.
Recent work has begun to explore mechanism design specifically for LLM-based systems: Duetting et al.~\cite{duetting2024mechanism} investigate auction and incentive design in settings where LLM agents interact strategically, while Horton~\cite{horton2023homo} demonstrates that LLMs can simulate economic agents whose behavior aligns with classical game-theoretic predictions---supporting our modeling assumption that agent behavior can be precisely captured by Bayesian game theory.
We extend tokenomics and mechanism design beyond financial systems to agent ecosystems, where tokens incentivize knowledge validation and honest service provision.


\textbf{Trust, Reliability, and Alignment in AI Systems.}
LLM hallucination---generating plausible but incorrect outputs---has been extensively documented~\cite{huang2023survey, ji2023hallucination, zhang2023siren} and remains a fundamental challenge for any system that depends on LLM-generated content.
While most mitigation strategies focus on individual model improvements (e.g., retrieval augmentation, self-consistency checking, chain-of-thought verification), our approach tackles reliability at the \textit{ecosystem level}: rather than trusting any single agent, we aggregate assessments from multiple architecturally heterogeneous validators under economic incentives that promote truthful evaluation.
This connects to the broader challenge of multi-agent alignment~\cite{de2023emergent}---ensuring that a population of self-interested agents collectively produces beneficial outcomes.
Traditional alignment research focuses on training individual models to follow human intent (e.g., via RLHF); our game-theoretic framework addresses alignment at a different level of abstraction: through mechanism design that makes honest behavior the individually rational strategy for every participant, regardless of each agent's internal training objective.
Kapoor et al.~\cite{kapoor2024agents} argue that rigorous evaluation of AI agent capabilities is essential for building reliable systems---a perspective that resonates with our PoI mechanism, which provides exactly such rigorous, decentralized, and incentive-compatible evaluation.
