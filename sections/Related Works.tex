\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

Our work draws from and extends several active research areas spanning LLM agents, decentralized AI infrastructure, mechanism design and strategic behavior, and trust/verification in AI-generated content. We survey each briefly and position our contributions.

\noindent\textbf{LLM Agent Systems.} Foundational frameworks advance individual agent capability, including ReAct~\cite{yao2023react} (reasoning interleaved with actions), Toolformer~\cite{schick2024toolformer} (self-supervised tool acquisition), HuggingGPT~\cite{shen2024hugginggpt} (cross-modal model orchestration), ToolLLM~\cite{qin2024toolllm} (API tool use at scale), Voyager~\cite{wang2023voyager} (lifelong learning), Reflexion~\cite{shinn2023reflexion}, and Self-Refine~\cite{madaan2023selfrefine} (self-improvement through reflection). Agent infrastructure (AIOS~\cite{mei2024aios}), benchmarks (AgentBench~\cite{liu2024agentbench}), and surveys~\cite{xi2023rise, li2024survey, wang2024survey_agents} support systematic study of the rapidly expanding landscape. Multi-agent frameworks extend these to collaborative task solving, including AutoGen~\cite{wu2023autogen}, MetaGPT~\cite{hong2023metagpt}, CAMEL~\cite{li2023camel}, ChatDev~\cite{qian2023communicative}, AgentVerse~\cite{chen2023agentverse}, CompeteAI~\cite{zhao2023competeai}, Generative Agents~\cite{park2023generative}, and deliberation or debate approaches~\cite{du2024debate, zhuge2024gptswarm, zhang2024proagent, chan2024chateval, zhang2024cooperative_embodied}. These frameworks assume cooperative agents under a central orchestrator or within a single organization, leaving the strategic self-interest and cross-organizational trust of open agent societies unaddressed.

\noindent\textbf{Decentralized AI Infrastructure.} Blockchain platforms such as Ethereum~\cite{buterin2014ethereum} and data availability layers~\cite{celestia2025data} provide the substrate for programmable coordination. AI-specific projects build token-incentivized infrastructure and marketplaces, including Bittensor~\cite{bittensor2023} (peer-to-peer intelligence market), Petals~\cite{borzunov2023petals} (distributed inference), SingularityNET~\cite{goertzel2017singularitynet}, Ocean Protocol~\cite{ocean2019protocol}, and Fetch.AI~\cite{fetch2019ai} (decentralized AI and data marketplaces), alongside recent agent-specific platforms such as Virtuals Protocol~\cite{virtuals2024}, ElizaOS~\cite{walters2025eliza}, and Olas~\cite{olas2024valory} that tokenize agents as on-chain economic actors. Surveys~\cite{yuan2024decentralized_inference, ding2024deai} and blockchain-based federated learning~\cite{hu2024blockchain_fl} further address distributed LLM serving and verifiable aggregation. These systems provide infrastructure and marketplaces but lack formal game-theoretic guarantees for cross-organizational agent coordination, which our ecosystem contributes at the mechanism-design layer.

\noindent\textbf{Mechanism Design and Strategic Behavior.} Tokenomics research~\cite{ito2024cryptoeconomics, abadi2024governance, zuo2023set} grounds our incentive design, and information-elicitation mechanisms such as prediction markets~\cite{wolfers2004prediction} and peer prediction~\cite{miller2005eliciting} provide theoretical precedents for truthful reporting without ground truth. A growing literature examines the strategic behavior of LLM agents~\cite{duetting2024mechanism, horton2023homo, brookins2023gpt_games, akata2023repeated_games, fish2024collusion, conitzer2024social_choice}, showing that LLM agents approximate rational play and can even learn to collude. We extend tokenomics and mechanism design to agent ecosystems, adding formal IC, IR, and BNE guarantees tailored to the three-role interaction structure.

\noindent\textbf{Trust and Verification.} LLM hallucination~\cite{huang2023survey, ji2023hallucination, zhang2023siren} has motivated a multi-agent verification line of work, including LLM-as-judge~\cite{zheng2023judging, kim2024prometheus}, self-consistency checks~\cite{manakul2023selfcheckgpt, wang2023selfconsistency, dhuliawala2023chainofverification, mundler2024selfcontradictory}, cross-examination~\cite{cohen2023crossexam}, and debate-based evaluation~\cite{chan2024chateval}; Kapoor et al.~\cite{kapoor2024agents} further argue that rigorous evaluation is essential for reliable agent systems. These approaches verify at the individual or small-group level. Our ecosystem-level PoI mechanism aggregates architecturally heterogeneous validators under economic incentives, making cross-verification incentive-compatible.
