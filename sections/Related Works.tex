\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

\noindent\textbf{Multi-Agent LLM Systems.} LLM-based multi-agent systems have rapidly expanded in scope~\cite{xi2023rise, wang2024survey_agents}. Cooperative frameworks such as AutoGen~\cite{wu2023autogen}, MetaGPT~\cite{hong2023metagpt}, CAMEL~\cite{li2023camel}, ChatDev~\cite{qian2023communicative}, and AgentVerse~\cite{chen2023agentverse} have agents collaborate on specialized subtasks under a shared orchestrator, while competitive and social-simulation settings such as CompeteAI~\cite{zhao2023competeai} and Generative Agents~\cite{park2023generative} study strategic interaction inside a single-author sandbox. A separate thread on deliberation and debate~\cite{du2024debate, zhuge2024gptswarm, zhang2024proagent, chan2024chateval, zhang2024cooperative_embodied} treats multi-agent exchange as an aggregation tool for evaluation. Across all three threads, agents share one designer, one identity registry, and one settlement boundary, leaving the cross-organizational trust problem of an open agent society unaddressed.

\noindent\textbf{Trust and Verification.} The reliability of LLM output is threatened by hallucination~\cite{huang2023survey, ji2023hallucination, zhang2023siren}, prompting a line of multi-agent verification work that includes LLM-as-judge~\cite{zheng2023judging, kim2024prometheus} and cross-examination~\cite{cohen2023crossexam}. Single-model self-consistency and self-check~\cite{manakul2023selfcheckgpt, wang2023selfconsistency, dhuliawala2023chainofverification, mundler2024selfcontradictory} extract verification signals by re-querying the same architecture that produced the output, offering limited defense against correlated errors. In parallel, reputation systems such as EigenTrust~\cite{kamvar2003eigentrust} aggregate peer feedback into trust scores but remain exposed to Sybil attacks~\cite{douceur2002sybil} whenever identities are cheap to mint. Our PoI mechanism closes both failure modes at once: architectural heterogeneity across validators breaks correlated errors, while staked capital replaces cheap identity as the gatekeeper of participation, pricing adversarial behavior at a tunable economic budget.

\noindent\textbf{Decentralized AI Infrastructure.} Blockchain platforms provide decentralized coordination through two primitives, a tamper-evident distributed ledger and a cryptoeconomic consensus rule, with Bitcoin's Proof-of-Work~\cite{nakamoto2008bitcoin} and Ethereum's Proof-of-Stake~\cite{buterin2017casper} showing that token incentives can secure adversarial participation without a central authority. Programmable smart contracts~\cite{buterin2014ethereum} extended this substrate to arbitrary applications, and AI-specific platforms built on top: Bittensor~\cite{bittensor2023}, SingularityNET~\cite{goertzel2017singularitynet}, Ocean Protocol~\cite{ocean2019protocol}, and Fetch.AI~\cite{fetch2019ai} operate token-incentivized inference and data marketplaces, while more recent projects such as Virtuals Protocol~\cite{virtuals2024}, ElizaOS~\cite{walters2025eliza}, and Olas~\cite{olas2024valory} tokenize individual agents as on-chain economic actors. Decentralized oracle networks~\cite{breidenbach2021chainlink} supply a related primitive, piping external data on-chain through staked reporters. What this ecosystem provides is infrastructure and marketplaces~\cite{ding2024deai}; what it still lacks is a formal game-theoretic backbone for cross-organizational agent coordination, which our mechanism-design layer supplies through token-flow and incentive-compatibility guarantees.

\noindent\textbf{Mechanism Design and Strategic Behavior.} Markets with information asymmetry are prone to adverse selection~\cite{akerlof1970market}, and costly signals can separate types at equilibrium~\cite{spence1973job}. This signaling logic underpins the producer self-screening in our ecosystem. Tokenomics research~\cite{ito2024cryptoeconomics, abadi2024governance, zuo2023set} further informs our incentive design, while information-elicitation mechanisms including prediction markets~\cite{wolfers2004prediction}, peer prediction~\cite{miller2005eliciting, dasgupta2013crowdsourced}, and the Bayesian Truth Serum~\cite{prelec2004bayesian} show that truthful reporting can be elicited even without ground truth. Empirically, LLM agents have been shown to approximate rational play and even learn to collude in repeated interaction~\cite{duetting2024mechanism, horton2023homo, brookins2023gpt_games, akata2023repeated_games, fish2024collusion, conitzer2024social_choice}, underscoring the need for incentive-compatible design rather than behavioral alignment alone. We integrate these threads into a single three-role ecosystem and establish formal incentive compatibility, individual rationality, and Bayesian--Nash equilibrium guarantees over the validator, producer, and consumer interactions of an open agent society under incomplete information.
