\subsection{Derivation of the Matching Probability $P_{\text{match}}$}
\label{app:pmatch_derivation}

We derive $P_{\text{match}}(n, q_v)$, the probability that a truthful validator's vote matches the majority when all $n$ validators report truthfully. Let the ground truth state of a task be $\theta \in \{0, 1\}$. Under Assumption~\ref{assum:binary}, each truthful validator's report agrees with $\theta$ independently with probability $q_v$.

A truthful validator $v_i$ matches the majority if and only if \textit{at least} $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor$ of the \textit{other} $n-1$ validators agree with $v_i$'s report. We condition on whether $v_i$'s report is correct:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Case 1: $v_i$ reports correctly} (probability $q_v$). The validator matches the majority iff $\geq \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$ of the other $n-1$ validators also report correctly:
    \[
    \alpha(n, q_v) = \sum_{k=\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}^{n-1} \binom{n-1}{k} q_v^k (1 - q_v)^{n-1-k}.
    \]

    \item \textbf{Case 2: $v_i$ reports incorrectly} (probability $1 - q_v$). The validator matches the majority iff $\geq \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$ of the other $n-1$ validators also report incorrectly:
    \[
    \beta(n, q_v) = \sum_{k=\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}^{n-1} \binom{n-1}{k} (1 - q_v)^k q_v^{n-1-k}.
    \]
\end{itemize}

The total matching probability is:
\begin{equation}
\tag{\ref{eq:pmatch}}
P_{\text{match}}(n, q_v) = q_v \cdot \alpha(n, q_v) + (1 - q_v) \cdot \beta(n, q_v).
\end{equation}


\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:pmatch_properties} (Properties of $P_{\text{match}}$)}
\label{app:pmatch_properties}

\textbf{(i) $P_{\text{match}}(n, q_v) > \frac{1}{2}$ for all $q_v > \frac{1}{2}$.}
$\alpha(n, q_v)$ and $\beta(n, q_v)$ are cumulative binomial tail probabilities with parameters $q_v$ and $1-q_v$ respectively, over the same threshold $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor$ out of $n-1$ trials.
Since $q_v > 1 - q_v$ when $q_v > \frac{1}{2}$, first-order stochastic dominance gives $\alpha > \beta$.
Hence $P_{\text{match}} = q_v \alpha + (1-q_v)\beta > q_v \beta + (1-q_v)\beta = \beta$.
To show $P_{\text{match}} > \frac{1}{2}$: since the full committee of $n$ validators forms an i.i.d.\ majority vote with individual accuracy $q_v > \frac{1}{2}$, the Condorcet jury theorem guarantees that the majority is correct with probability exceeding $q_v > \frac{1}{2}$. A truthful $v_i$ agrees with a correct majority with probability $q_v$ and with an incorrect majority with probability $1-q_v$, giving $P_{\text{match}} > q_v^2 + (1-q_v)^2 > \frac{1}{2}$ for all $q_v \neq \frac{1}{2}$.

\textbf{(ii) $P_{\text{match}}$ is strictly increasing in $q_v$ and in $n$.}
For fixed $n$, increasing $q_v$ simultaneously raises $\alpha$ (more likely to be in the correct majority) and reduces the weight on the smaller term $\beta$, strictly increasing $P_{\text{match}}$.
For fixed $q_v > \frac{1}{2}$, increasing $n$ strengthens the majority (by the law of large numbers), making the majority more likely to be correct and thus increasing the probability that any individual truthful vote matches it.

\textbf{(iii) $P_{\text{match}} \to 1$ as $n \to \infty$.}
Under truthful play with $q_v > \frac{1}{2}$, the fraction of correct votes converges to $q_v > \frac{1}{2}$ almost surely by the strong law of large numbers. The majority is correct with probability $\to 1$, and a truthful voter matches this correct majority with probability $\to q_v \cdot 1 + (1-q_v) \cdot 0 = q_v$. Since $q_v > \frac{1}{2}$ and majority correctness $\to 1$, we get $P_{\text{match}} \to 1$.


\subsection{Free-Riding Matching Probability}
\label{app:freeriding}

We show that a random vote matches the majority with probability exactly $\frac{1}{2}$. Condition on the ground truth $\theta$ and the majority outcome $M$ of the other $n-1$ truthful validators. Since $v_i$'s random vote is independent of both $\theta$ and the other validators' reports, and since $r_i$ takes each value with probability $\frac{1}{2}$:
\[
\Pr(r_i = M) = \Pr(r_i = 1)\Pr(M = 1) + \Pr(r_i = 0)\Pr(M = 0) = \tfrac{1}{2}\Pr(M=1) + \tfrac{1}{2}\Pr(M=0) = \tfrac{1}{2}.
\]
This holds regardless of the distribution of $M$, because the random vote is uniformly distributed and independent of everything else.


\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{Strict IC for Validators} (Validator IC)}
\label{app:proof_ic}

Truthful effort strictly dominates free-riding iff $\mathbb{E}[U \mid e=1] > \mathbb{E}[U \mid e=0]$. Substituting the expected payoffs:
\begin{align}
&P_{\text{match}} \cdot R_{\min} - (1 - P_{\text{match}}) \cdot \sigma - c_u > \frac{1}{2}(R_{\min} - \sigma). \nonumber
\end{align}
Expanding the left-hand side and collecting terms involving $P_{\text{match}}$:
\begin{align}
\Longleftrightarrow\quad & P_{\text{match}} \cdot R_{\min} + P_{\text{match}} \cdot \sigma - \sigma - c_u > \frac{1}{2} R_{\min} - \frac{1}{2}\sigma. \nonumber
\end{align}
Rearranging:
\begin{align}
\Longleftrightarrow\quad & P_{\text{match}} (R_{\min} + \sigma) - \frac{1}{2}(R_{\min} + \sigma) > c_u. \nonumber
\end{align}
Factoring out $(R_{\min} + \sigma)$:
\begin{align}
\Longleftrightarrow\quad & (R_{\min} + \sigma)\Big(P_{\text{match}} - \frac{1}{2}\Big) > c_u.
\end{align}
Since $R_{\min} = \tau_{\text{val}} + \sigma$, we obtain $R_{\min} + \sigma = \tau_{\text{val}} + 2\sigma$, yielding the stated condition~\eqref{eq:ic}. \qed


\subsection{MLRP as a Consequence of the Binary Signal Model}
\label{app:mlrp_derived}

Assumption~\ref{assum:mlrp} follows from Assumptions~\ref{assum:validators}--\ref{assum:costs}. Each validator independently compares its own output (correct with probability $q_v$) against the producer's output (correct with probability $q_p$). On a given task, validator $v_i$ reports ``match'' ($r_i = 1$) if both are correct or both incorrect, and ``mismatch'' otherwise. The per-task agreement probability is:
\[
p_{\text{agree}}(q_p, q_v) = q_p q_v + (1 - q_p)(1 - q_v).
\]
This is strictly increasing in $q_p$ for any fixed $q_v > \frac{1}{2}$ (since $\frac{\partial}{\partial q_p} p_{\text{agree}} = 2q_v - 1 > 0$).
The consensus $Z = 1$ (pass) requires that a strict majority of validators report ``match.'' Since each validator's agreement probability is strictly increasing in $q_p$, the aggregate pass probability under majority voting is itself strictly increasing and continuous in $q_p$, establishing the MLRP. \qed


\subsection{Extension from Single Query to $m$ Queries}
\label{app:m_extension}

The IC analysis derives conditions on a per-query basis ($m = 1$) without loss of generality. Since tasks are drawn i.i.d.\ (Assumption~\ref{assum:binary}), the validator's effort decision on each task is an independent subgame with identical structure. If truthful effort is a strict best response on each individual task, it remains so across all $m$ tasks, because the expected payoff from exerting effort on all tasks is $m$ times the per-task payoff gain. Formally, the IC condition for $m$ tasks scales to $m(\tau_{\text{val}} + 2\sigma)(P_{\text{match}} - \frac{1}{2}) > m \cdot c_u$, which simplifies to the same per-task condition. The producer and consumer IR conditions similarly aggregate linearly across tasks.


\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{IR for Producers} (Producer IR)}
\label{app:proof_producer_ir}

The producer promotes iff $\Pi_p(q_p) \geq 0$. By Equation~\eqref{eq:producer_payoff}:
\[
V_p(q_p) \cdot (\tau_s - c_s) \geq \tau_p + mn\tau_{\text{val}}.
\]
The left-hand side is the product of $V_p(q_p)$---strictly increasing and continuous in $q_p$ by Assumption~\ref{assum:mlrp}---and the positive constant $(\tau_s - c_s)$, hence is itself strictly increasing and continuous in $q_p$.

At $q_p \to \frac{1}{2}$ (minimal quality), $V_p(\frac{1}{2})$ is small enough that $V_p(\frac{1}{2}) \cdot (\tau_s - c_s) < \tau_p + mn\tau_{\text{val}}$ by the cost ordering in Assumption~\ref{assum:costs}, so $\Pi_p < 0$.
At $q_p \to 1$, $V_p(q_p) \to 1$, so $\Pi_p = (\tau_s - c_s) - (\tau_p + mn\tau_{\text{val}}) > 0$ by $mn\tau_{\text{val}} \ll \tau_p \ll \tau_s$.

By the intermediate value theorem, there exists a unique $\bar{q}_p$ satisfying $V_p(\bar{q}_p) \cdot (\tau_s - c_s) = \tau_p + mn\tau_{\text{val}}$, and by strict monotonicity, $\Pi_p(q_p) \geq 0 \iff q_p \geq \bar{q}_p$. \qed


\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{IR-based Policy for Consumers} (Consumer IR)}
\label{app:proof_consumer_ir}

The consumer subscribes iff $\Pi_c \geq 0$, i.e., the posterior expected utility exceeds the total cost $C_c \coloneqq \tau_s + mn\tau_{\text{val}}$.

\textit{Step 1: Monotonicity.}
Fix two quality levels $q_p' > q_p$.
By Assumption~\ref{assum:mlrp}, $V(q_p') > V(q_p)$, so the pass probability is higher for the better producer.
The MLRP implies that the posterior distribution $f(\cdot \mid Z = 1)$ under $q_p'$ first-order stochastically dominates that under $q_p$.
Since $V_c(\cdot)$ is strictly increasing, $\mathbb{E}[V_c \mid Z = 1, q_p'] > \mathbb{E}[V_c \mid Z = 1, q_p]$.

\textit{Step 2: Boundary behavior.}
When $q_p \to \frac{1}{2}$, the posterior expected utility is bounded above by $V_c(\frac{1}{2}) + \epsilon$ for small $\epsilon$, and $\Pi_c < 0$.
When $q_p \to 1$, $\mathbb{E}[V_c \mid Z = 1] \to V_c(1) \gg C_c$, giving $\Pi_c > 0$.

\textit{Step 3: Existence and uniqueness.}
The posterior expected utility is strictly increasing and continuous, transitioning from negative to positive. By the intermediate value theorem, there exists a unique $\bar{q}_c$ where $\Pi_c = 0$, and $\Pi_c \geq 0 \iff q_p \geq \bar{q}_c$.

\textit{Step 4: Signal-contingent implementation.}
The consumer conditions on $Z$ rather than observing $q_p$ directly. When $Z = 1$, the posterior $f(q_p \mid Z = 1)$ upweights high-quality producers (by the MLRP), and the condition $\mathbb{E}[V_c(q_p) \mid Z = 1] \geq C_c$ is satisfied for producers with $q_p \geq \bar{q}_c$, because validation is informative enough to distinguish quality levels above and below the threshold in expectation. \qed


\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{Symmetric Bayesian Nash Equilibrium} (BNE)}
\label{app:proof_bne}

\textit{Part 1: Non-emptiness of the feasible parameter region.}
For V-IC, fix $q_v > \frac{1}{2}$ and $n \geq 3$. By Proposition~\ref{prop:pmatch_properties}(i), $P_{\text{match}} - \frac{1}{2} > 0$, so $\tau_{\text{val}}$ and $\sigma$ can be chosen large enough to satisfy $(\tau_{\text{val}} + 2\sigma)(P_{\text{match}} - \frac{1}{2}) > c_u$.
Concretely: with $n = 5$, $q_v = 0.8$, $P_{\text{match}}(5, 0.8) \approx 0.942$, giving $(\tau_{\text{val}} + 2\sigma) \times 0.442$. Setting $\tau_{\text{val}} = \sigma = 1$ yields $1.326 > c_u$ for any $c_u < 1$.

Given V-IC, the thresholds $\bar{q}_p$ and $\bar{q}_c$ are well-defined. By comparative statics, they can be made arbitrarily close to $\frac{1}{2}$ via sufficiently large $\tau_s$ and informative validation. Since $F$ has support on $(\frac{1}{2}, 1]$, P-IR and C-IR are satisfiable.

\textit{Part 2: Verification of best responses.}
\textit{Validators}: Given $n-1$ truthful others, IC (Theorem~\ref{Strict IC for Validators}) ensures truthful effort is strictly optimal.
\textit{Producers}: Given informative $Z$, promoting iff $q_p \geq \bar{q}_p$ maximizes payoff (Theorem~\ref{IR for Producers}).
\textit{Consumers}: Given informative $Z$ and pre-screened producers, subscribing when $\mathbb{E}[V_c(q_p) \mid Z = 1] \geq C_c$ maximizes payoff (Theorem~\ref{IR-based Policy for Consumers}).
\textit{Symmetry}: All agents of the same type face identical payoff structures, so optimal strategies are identical within each type.

Unilateral deviation by any agent strictly reduces its payoff by the strict inequalities in Theorems~\ref{Strict IC for Validators}--\ref{IR-based Policy for Consumers}. \qed


\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:sybil_ic} (IC under Sybil Attacks)}
\label{app:proof_sybil}

The proof mirrors Theorem~\ref{Strict IC for Validators}; only $P_{\text{match}}$ changes. The honest validator's payoff structure is identical: stake $\sigma$, earn $R_{\min}$ if matching majority, forfeit $\sigma$ otherwise. The shirking payoff $\frac{1}{2}(R_{\min} - \sigma)$ is unchanged (a random vote matches any majority with probability $\frac{1}{2}$, independent of adversarial votes; see Appendix~\ref{app:freeriding}).

It remains to show $P_{\text{match}}^{\text{Sybil}} > \frac{1}{2}$. Condition on $\theta$. When $\theta$ agrees with the adversary's vote direction, the $k$ adversarial votes reinforce truth, giving $P_{\text{match}}^{\text{Sybil}} \geq P_{\text{match}}$. When $\theta$ opposes the adversary, the honest validators ($h > n/2$) must overcome $k$ adversarial votes. By the Condorcet jury theorem applied to the $h$ honest validators, their majority is correct with probability $> \frac{1}{2}$. A truthful $v_i$ agrees with this correct majority with probability $\geq q_v^2 + (1-q_v)^2 > \frac{1}{2}$.

Averaging over both cases yields $P_{\text{match}}^{\text{Sybil}} > \frac{1}{2}$. Substituting into the IC derivation yields condition~\eqref{eq:ic_sybil}. \qed
