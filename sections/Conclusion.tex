\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The decentralized agent society demands a coordination principle in which honest, high-quality participation emerges as the individually rational choice for every agent, without reliance on any centralized authority. This paper supplies such a principle through a tokenomics-driven ecosystem organized around three dynamically interchangeable roles, a Proof-of-Intelligence consensus that binds validator effort to economic stake, and a self-reinforcing tokenomics-knowledge dual loop that aligns individual incentives with collective welfare. Within a unified Bayesian game framework, we prove that these design choices sustain a symmetric Bayesian--Nash equilibrium in which cooperation, accountability, and verifiability, the three pillars undermined by the trust crisis, emerge as equilibrium outcomes rather than externally imposed constraints, and that this equilibrium remains robust to bounded Sybil attacks, residual validator correlation, and heterogeneous validator quality, with the economic stake serving as a tunable parameter against adversarial degradation. The DeQA case study corroborates these theoretical predictions under realistic multi-agent simulation, confirming that the mechanism drives agent communities toward truthful participation, effective quality screening, and rising social welfare. Taken together, these results establish that the problem of trust in the decentralized agent society can be systematically reduced to the problem of incentive design, offering a principled foundation for cross-organizational agent collaboration grounded in economic rationality.
