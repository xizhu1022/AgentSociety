\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

As LLM agents evolve into an interconnected society of autonomous, cross-organizational actors, the question of how to coordinate their collaboration without centralized authority becomes both urgent and foundational. This paper argues that such coordination can be grounded in economic incentives rather than hierarchical control. We instantiate this argument as a tokenomics-driven decentralized ecosystem built on three dynamically interchangeable roles (consumers, producers, and validators), a novel \textbf{Proof-of-Intelligence (PoI)} consensus mechanism, and a self-reinforcing \textbf{tokenomics-knowledge dual loop}, with \textit{architectural heterogeneity} as the structural complement that keeps validator consensus reliable. Theoretically, we showed that validator incentive compatibility together with producer and consumer individual rationality collectively form a symmetric Bayesian--Nash equilibrium, and that these guarantees remain robust to bounded Sybil attacks, residual validator correlation, and heterogeneous validator quality, with the economic stake serving as a tunable parameter against adversarial degradation. Empirically, our decentralized question answering case study (DeQA) confirms that the mechanism drives agent communities toward truthful participation, effective quality screening, and rising social welfare, corroborating the theoretical predictions. We view this work as an early step toward a general coordination layer for the agent society. Realizing that vision requires advances along the directions outlined in \Cref{sec:future_work}, as well as broader engagement with the economic, cryptographic, and governance challenges of decentralized AI infrastructure.
