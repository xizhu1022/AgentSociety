\begin{abstract}

As Large Language Models mature into autonomous LLM-based agents deployed by independent organizations, the emerging landscape signifies a fundamental transition toward an open agent society, a decentralized population of specialized agents that must discover, evaluate, and transact across organizational boundaries. Its realization, however, faces a fundamental trust crisis. Agents may produce hallucinated outputs due to capability limitations, or engage in strategic misbehavior such as shirking effort and misrepresenting competence. The simultaneous collapse of three foundational pillars, cooperation, accountability, and verifiability, triggers an adverse selection spiral that threatens to unravel the agent society into a ``market for lemons''. Centralized orchestration cannot resolve this crisis, as it introduces systemic pathologies including data monopoly, routing manipulation, and loss of self-governance.

To address this trust crisis, we propose an incentive-compatible decentralized ecosystem where autonomous agents coordinate through economic incentives without central authority. Agents assume three dynamically interchangeable roles, consumers issuing token-backed service requests, producers providing knowledge-intensive services, and validators assessing service quality. Inspired by Proof-of-Work and Proof-of-Stake, we define this validation paradigm as Proof-of-Intelligence (PoI), in which heterogeneous validators exert effort and vote truthfully to form a high-fidelity consensus. Validators stake tokens and receive rewards for judgments aligned with the majority consensus, while those deviating forfeit their stake. A key design principle is architectural heterogeneity, requiring validators to employ model architectures distinct from the producer to reduce correlated errors and approximate the independence assumption behind reliable consensus. Token flows across all three roles form a self-reinforcing tokenomics-knowledge dual loop, the primary driving force behind the prosperity of the ecosystem.

Within a unified game-theoretic framework, we establish validator incentive compatibility (V-IC), derive threshold-optimal promotion and subscription policies under individual rationality (P-IR and C-IR), and prove the existence of a symmetric Bayesian--Nash equilibrium in which no agent has incentive to unilaterally deviate. These guarantees remain robust to bounded Sybil attacks, residual validator correlation, and heterogeneous validator quality, with the economic stake serving as a tunable parameter that compensates for adversarial degradation. The dual loop further generates evolutionary selection pressure, progressively filtering out low-quality agents and elevating the aggregate welfare of the agent society. We instantiate the framework as a decentralized question answering system (DeQA) and demonstrate through extensive simulation that the mechanism drives agent communities toward truthful participation, rigorous quality screening, and improved social welfare.

\end{abstract}
